# Chapter 16: Genetics and the Brain

## 16.1 Heritability of Brain Traits

Twin and family studies have established that virtually all brain-related traits have a significant genetic component:

| Trait | Heritability Estimate | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|
| General intelligence (g) | 50–80% | Increases with age (from ~40% in childhood to ~80% in adulthood); shared environment decreases in influence |
| Personality (Big Five) | 40–60% | Extraversion ~50%, neuroticism ~48%, openness ~57%, agreeableness ~42%, conscientiousness ~49% |
| Brain volume (total) | ~80–90% | Among the most heritable human traits |
| Cortical thickness | 60–80% | Varies by region; frontal cortex moderately heritable; parietal highly heritable |
| White matter integrity (FA) | 50–80% | Tract-specific variation |
| Schizophrenia | ~80% | Concordance: ~48% monozygotic vs. ~6% dizygotic twins |
| Bipolar disorder | ~70–80% | Strong genetic overlap with schizophrenia |
| Major depression | ~35–40% | Lower heritability; substantial environmental component |
| Autism spectrum disorder | ~80–90% | Among the highest heritability of psychiatric conditions |
| ADHD | ~70–80% | Substantial genetic overlap with other traits (e.g., educational attainment) |
| Alcohol use disorder | ~50–60% | Gene × environment interaction prominent |

**Important caveats**: Heritability is a population statistic, not a measure of genetic determinism for individuals. High heritability does not mean fixed or immutable — height is ~80% heritable but has increased dramatically with improved nutrition. Heritability estimates are population- and environment-specific.

## 16.2 The Genetic Architecture of Brain Traits

### From Mendelian to Polygenic

**Monogenic (Mendelian) neurological disorders**: Single gene mutations with large effects — Huntington's disease (HTT), phenylketonuria (PAH), Fragile X (FMR1), many rare intellectual disabilities, early-onset familial Alzheimer's (APP, PSEN1, PSEN2). These are rare but provide profound insight into molecular mechanisms.

**Polygenic architecture** (the norm for complex brain traits): Most brain-related traits — intelligence, personality, psychiatric disorders — are influenced by **thousands of genetic variants**, each with tiny effect size (typically <0.01% of variance per variant). This was revealed by genome-wide association studies (GWAS):

- **Intelligence/educational attainment**: GWAS have identified >4,000 associated loci. Each explains a fraction of a percent of variance. Polygenic scores (summing the effects of many variants) can now explain ~15% of variance in educational attainment — significant but far from predictive for individuals.
- **Schizophrenia**: ~300+ genome-wide significant loci identified; polygenic architecture with thousands of contributing variants. Combined polygenic scores explain ~7% of variance on the liability scale.
- **Height analogy**: ~10,000 variants explain ~40% of variance in height. Brain traits are similarly (or more) complex.

### Common vs. Rare Variants

**Common variant, small effect** (GWAS detectable): The bulk of heritable variation for most traits. Individually tiny effects but collectively explain most genetic risk.

**Rare variant, large effect**: De novo mutations (spontaneous in the individual, not inherited), copy number variants (CNVs), and rare coding mutations. Particularly important in:
- **Autism**: ~100+ high-confidence risk genes identified; ~10–20% of cases involve identifiable de novo mutations or CNVs (e.g., 16p11.2 deletion, SHANK3, CHD8, SCN2A)
- **Intellectual disability**: De novo mutations in hundreds of genes
- **Schizophrenia**: Rare CNVs (22q11.2 deletion → ~25× increased risk) supplement common variant risk

### Missing Heritability

GWAS-identified variants typically explain only 20–50% of twin-estimated heritability. The "missing heritability" likely resides in:
- Rare variants below GWAS detection thresholds
- Gene-gene interactions (epistasis)
- Gene-environment interactions (GxE)
- Structural variants (inversions, translocations) poorly captured by SNP arrays
- Non-additive genetic effects
- Overestimated twin heritability (shared environment confounds)

## 16.3 Key Gene Families in Brain Function

### Ion Channels (Channelopathies)

Mutations in ion channel genes cause epilepsy, migraine, ataxia, and pain disorders:

| Gene | Channel | Associated Disorder |
|---|---|---|
| SCN1A | Nav1.1 (sodium) | Dravet syndrome (severe childhood epilepsy) |
| SCN2A | Nav1.2 (sodium) | Epileptic encephalopathy, autism |
| SCN9A | Nav1.7 (sodium) | Congenital insensitivity to pain (loss-of-function); erythromelalgia (gain-of-function) |
| KCNQ2/3 | Kv7.2/7.3 (potassium) | Benign familial neonatal epilepsy |
| CACNA1A | Cav2.1 (calcium) | Familial hemiplegic migraine, episodic ataxia type 2, spinocerebellar ataxia type 6 |
| GRIN2A/2B | GluN2A/2B (NMDA) | Epileptic encephalopathy, intellectual disability |

### Neurotransmitter System Genes

- **SLC6A4** (SERT — serotonin transporter): The 5-HTTLPR polymorphism (short allele → reduced SERT expression) was proposed to moderate stress–depression association. Initial findings (Caspi et al., 2003) were highly influential but subsequent meta-analyses yielded mixed results. The gene likely has small effects that interact with environmental context in complex ways.
- **COMT** (Val158Met): Val allele → higher COMT activity → lower PFC dopamine → better stress resilience but slightly lower working memory; Met allele → lower COMT activity → higher PFC dopamine → better working memory but higher anxiety vulnerability ("warrior vs. worrier" — oversimplified but illustrative).
- **DRD4** (dopamine D4 receptor): 7-repeat VNTR allele associated with novelty-seeking and ADHD (effect size small).
- **MAOA** ("warrior gene"): Low-activity allele + childhood maltreatment → increased antisocial behavior risk (Caspi et al., 2002). Effect is probabilistic, not deterministic; strongly influenced by environment.

### Neurotrophic Factors

- **BDNF** (brain-derived neurotrophic factor): Critical for neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, and adult neurogenesis. Val66Met polymorphism: Met allele → impaired activity-dependent BDNF secretion → reduced hippocampal volume, impaired episodic memory, altered extinction learning. Met carriers show altered response to exercise's cognitive benefits.
- **NGF** (nerve growth factor): Basal forebrain cholinergic neuron survival. Reduced in AD.

### Synaptic Genes

- **SHANK1/2/3**: Postsynaptic scaffolding proteins at glutamatergic synapses. SHANK3 mutations → Phelan-McDermid syndrome (intellectual disability, autism, absent speech). SHANK genes are among the most commonly mutated in autism.
- **NRXN1** (neurexin) / **NLGN3/4** (neuroligin): Transsynaptic adhesion molecules. Mutations → autism, intellectual disability, schizophrenia.
- **DISC1** (Disrupted in Schizophrenia 1): Originally identified in a large Scottish family with high psychiatric illness burden. Involved in neuronal migration, synaptic plasticity, and mitochondrial function.
- **FMR1** (Fragile X): CGG repeat expansion → methylation and silencing of FMRP → loss of translational repression at synapses → excessive mGluR5-mediated LTD and protein synthesis → intellectual disability, autism features (most common inherited cause of intellectual disability).

## 16.4 Epigenetics and Brain Function

(See Chapter 11 for epigenetics in development.)

### Experience-Dependent Epigenetic Changes

**Stress and early life**: Maternal care in rats (licking and grooming) → increased hippocampal glucocorticoid receptor (Nr3c1) expression via demethylation of the promoter → better stress regulation in offspring (Meaney & Szyf). Human studies: Childhood abuse → increased Nr3c1 methylation in hippocampus → HPA axis dysregulation. These changes can persist into adulthood but are potentially reversible.

**Learning and memory**: Memory formation induces rapid epigenetic changes:
- DNA methylation changes at memory-related gene promoters (BDNF, reelin, PP1) within hours of learning
- Histone acetylation at learning-associated genes; HDAC inhibitors enhance memory consolidation
- DNA methylation in cortical neurons may serve as a maintenance mechanism for long-term memory storage (stable epigenetic marks in cortical engram cells)

**Addiction**: Drug exposure induces lasting epigenetic changes in reward circuitry:
- Cocaine → ΔFosB accumulation in NAc → altered histone acetylation at hundreds of genes → persistent changes in synaptic plasticity and drug responsiveness
- Histone modifications at BDNF and glutamate receptor genes in VTA and NAc → enhanced reward sensitivity

### Psychiatric Epigenetics

- **Schizophrenia**: Altered DNA methylation at GAD1 (GAD67 — GABA synthesis), RELN (reelin), COMT, and other loci in postmortem brain studies. MeCP2 (methyl-CpG binding protein) dysfunction → Rett syndrome (mutations) and possibly contributes to other neurodevelopmental disorders.
- **Depression**: BDNF promoter methylation, SLC6A4 methylation, and FKBP5 demethylation associated with MDD and treatment response.
- **PTSD**: Altered methylation of glucocorticoid receptor genes, FKBP5, and immune-related genes. Pre-existing epigenetic differences may confer vulnerability.

## 16.5 Pharmacogenomics and the Brain

Genetic variation influences drug response (efficacy and side effects) in neuropsychiatry:

### Cytochrome P450 Enzymes

Most psychotropic drugs are metabolized by liver CYP450 enzymes. Genetic polymorphisms determine metabolizer status:

| CYP Enzyme | Relevant Drugs | Clinical Impact |
|---|---|---|
| CYP2D6 | SSRIs, TCAs, codeine → morphine, tamoxifen, antipsychotics | Poor metabolizers (5–10% Caucasians): Drug accumulation, increased side effects. Ultra-rapid metabolizers (1–10%): Drug inefficacy. |
| CYP2C19 | Citalopram, escitalopram, clopidogrel, diazepam, omeprazole | Poor metabolizers (2–5% Caucasians, 12–23% East Asians): Elevated SSRI levels |
| CYP3A4/5 | Benzodiazepines, carbamazepine, many antipsychotics | Less polymorphic but drug interactions significant |
| CYP1A2 | Clozapine, olanzapine, theophylline | Induced by smoking; dose adjustments needed |

### HLA Genes and Drug Hypersensitivity

- **HLA-A*31:01**: Carbamazepine hypersensitivity (Stevens-Johnson syndrome) in Europeans
- **HLA-B*15:02**: Carbamazepine-induced SJS/TEN in Southeast Asian populations (FDA black box warning — test before prescribing)

### Pharmacogenomic-Guided Prescribing

The Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium (CPIC) provides genotype-based dosing guidelines for multiple psychotropics. Adoption is growing but not yet universal. Studies suggest pharmacogenomic-guided antidepressant selection may improve remission rates by 30–50% compared to treatment-as-usual (though evidence quality varies).

## 16.6 Gene-Environment Interaction (GxE)

Genes and environment do not operate independently — they interact:

**Diathesis-stress model**: Genetic vulnerability (diathesis) + environmental stressor → disease. Example: Short 5-HTTLPR allele + childhood adversity → increased depression risk (though the robustness of this specific GxE finding is debated).

**Differential susceptibility model** (Belsky): The same genetic variants that confer vulnerability under adverse conditions may confer enhanced benefit under supportive conditions. "Orchid" (sensitive) vs. "dandelion" (resilient) children. The 7-repeat DRD4 allele associated with worse outcomes in poor environments but better outcomes in enriched environments.

**Biological embedding**: Early environmental exposures (stress, nutrition, toxins, enrichment) become biologically embedded through epigenetic mechanisms, altering gene expression patterns that persist into adulthood — a molecular bridge between nature and nurture.
