# Chapter 15: The Aging Brain and Neurodegeneration

## 15.1 Normal Brain Aging

Aging produces widespread but regionally selective changes in brain structure and function:

### Structural Changes
- **Gray matter volume**: Declines ~0.2–0.5% per year after age 30, accelerating after 60. Prefrontal cortex and hippocampus show the steepest declines; primary sensory and motor cortices are relatively preserved.
- **White matter**: Volume increases until ~40–50 (continued myelination), then declines. White matter integrity (measured by diffusion tensor imaging — fractional anisotropy) decreases, particularly in anterior fiber tracts (anterior-to-posterior gradient of aging).
- **Ventricular enlargement**: CSF spaces expand as parenchymal volume decreases.
- **Cortical thinning**: ~0.01–0.03 mm/year in prefrontal regions.
- **Hippocampal volume**: Declines ~1–2% per year after age 50; accelerated in prodromal Alzheimer's disease.
- **Dopaminergic system**: ~6–10% loss of striatal dopamine D2 receptors and DAT binding per decade after age 20 → contributes to age-related slowing and executive function decline.

### Cellular and Molecular Changes
- **Neuronal loss**: Minimal in most cortical areas during normal aging (countering the old myth of massive daily neuron death); significant losses occur in specific regions: locus coeruleus (~25% by age 80), substantia nigra (~35%), nucleus basalis of Meynert.
- **Dendritic regression**: Reduced dendritic branching and spine density in PFC and hippocampal neurons.
- **Synaptic changes**: Decreased synapse density and reduced LTP magnitude.
- **Myelin degradation**: Oligodendrocyte dysfunction → myelin thinning, shorter internodes → slowed conduction velocity.
- **Oxidative stress**: Accumulated mitochondrial DNA mutations → reduced ATP production, increased ROS → oxidative damage to proteins, lipids, and DNA.
- **Telomere shortening**: Progressive with each cell division; associated with cellular senescence. Shortened telomeres in peripheral blood cells correlate with brain aging biomarkers.
- **Epigenetic drift**: Altered DNA methylation patterns, histone modifications → dysregulated gene expression.
- **Neuroinflammation**: Age-related microglial priming ("inflammaging") → exaggerated inflammatory response to stimuli; chronic low-grade neuroinflammation.
- **Proteostasis decline**: Reduced efficiency of proteasome, autophagy, and unfolded protein response → accumulation of misfolded proteins (even in normal aging — some amyloid and tau pathology is common in cognitively normal elderly).

### Cognitive Changes
- **Declines**: Processing speed (most consistent age-related change), episodic memory, source memory, working memory capacity, divided attention, executive function.
- **Preserved**: Semantic memory (vocabulary, general knowledge — may increase into the 70s), procedural memory, emotional regulation (positivity effect — older adults preferentially attend to and remember positive information), crystallized intelligence.
- **Compensatory mechanisms**: Older adults recruit bilateral prefrontal regions during tasks that younger adults perform with unilateral activation (HAROLD pattern — Hemispheric Asymmetry Reduction in Older Adults). PASA pattern (Posterior-Anterior Shift in Aging) — reduced posterior activity compensated by increased frontal activity.

### Cognitive Reserve

**Cognitive reserve** refers to the brain's resilience to pathology — the ability to maintain function despite accumulating neuropathology. High reserve allows individuals to tolerate more Alzheimer's pathology before showing symptoms.

**Sources of reserve**: Education, occupational complexity, bilingualism, social engagement, physical exercise, cognitively stimulating activities. Mechanisms: Larger neural networks, more efficient processing, ability to recruit compensatory circuits. Not "extra neurons" but more flexible and efficient use of existing networks.

## 15.2 Alzheimer's Disease (AD)

The most common neurodegenerative disease and cause of dementia (~60–80% of dementia cases). Estimated 50+ million cases worldwide; prevalence doubles every 5 years after age 65.

### Pathological Hallmarks

**Amyloid plaques**: Extracellular deposits of **amyloid-β (Aβ)** peptide (primarily Aβ42 and Aβ40).
- Aβ is generated by sequential cleavage of **amyloid precursor protein (APP)** by **β-secretase (BACE1)** and **γ-secretase** (a complex containing presenilin-1 or presenilin-2).
- The non-amyloidogenic pathway: APP cleaved by α-secretase → no Aβ formation.
- Aβ monomers → oligomers → protofibrils → fibrils → plaques. **Soluble Aβ oligomers** are now considered the most toxic species (more than plaques), disrupting synaptic function, inducing tau phosphorylation, and triggering inflammatory cascades.
- Plaque distribution: Initially in default mode network regions (medial PFC, PCC, precuneus), then spreads to neocortex. Detected in vivo by amyloid PET (Pittsburgh compound B, florbetapir).

**Neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs)**: Intraneuronal aggregates of hyperphosphorylated **tau protein**.
- Tau normally stabilizes microtubules in axons.
- Hyperphosphorylation → tau detaches from microtubules → self-aggregates into paired helical filaments → NFTs.
- Tau pathology correlates better with cognitive decline than amyloid plaques.
- Tau spreads in a stereotyped pattern (Braak staging): I–II: Transentorhinal cortex → III–IV: Limbic (hippocampus, amygdala) → V–VI: Neocortex. Spread occurs prion-like along connected circuits (trans-synaptic propagation of pathological tau seeds).

**Synaptic loss**: The strongest correlate of cognitive decline in AD. Synapse loss exceeds neuron loss, particularly in hippocampus and association cortex. Complement-mediated microglial synaptic pruning reactivated in AD (C1q → C3 tagging of synapses → CR3-mediated microglial engulfment).

**Cholinergic degeneration**: Loss of cholinergic neurons in the nucleus basalis of Meynert → reduced cortical ACh → attention and memory deficits. Basis for cholinesterase inhibitor therapy (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine — symptomatic, not disease-modifying).

### Genetics

**Early-onset familial AD** (<5% of cases, onset <65 years):
- **APP** mutations (chromosome 21) — alter Aβ production
- **PSEN1** (presenilin-1, chromosome 14) — most common familial AD mutations; increase Aβ42/Aβ40 ratio
- **PSEN2** (presenilin-2, chromosome 1) — rare
- Down syndrome (trisomy 21 → 3 copies of APP gene) → near-universal AD pathology by age 40

**Late-onset sporadic AD** (>95% of cases):
- **APOE ε4 allele**: The strongest genetic risk factor. APOE4 carriers: 3× (heterozygous) to 12× (homozygous) increased risk. Mechanism: impaired Aβ clearance, enhanced Aβ aggregation, reduced synaptic repair, altered lipid metabolism. APOE2 is protective (~0.6× risk).
- **TREM2 variants**: Impair microglial phagocytic function → reduced Aβ clearance; ~2–4× increased risk.
- >75 other risk loci identified by GWAS, many related to immune function (CD33, CR1, CLU), endosomal trafficking (BIN1, PICALM, SORL1), and lipid metabolism.

### Disease-Modifying Therapies (as of 2025)

Anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) have demonstrated modest but statistically significant slowing of cognitive decline (~25–35% slowing) in early-stage AD patients with confirmed amyloid positivity. Side effects include amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA — brain edema and microhemorrhages), more common in APOE4 carriers. These represent the first disease-modifying treatments but underscore that AD is likely driven by multiple converging pathologies beyond amyloid alone.

## 15.3 Parkinson's Disease (PD)

The second most common neurodegenerative disease (~1% prevalence over age 60; ~10 million cases worldwide).

### Pathology

**Dopaminergic neuron loss**: Progressive degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the **substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc)** → reduced dopamine in the dorsal striatum (putamen affected earlier than caudate). Motor symptoms appear when ~60–80% of striatal dopamine and ~30–50% of SNc neurons are lost — a long prodromal phase precedes clinical diagnosis.

**Lewy bodies and Lewy neurites**: Intraneuronal inclusions composed primarily of aggregated **α-synuclein** protein. α-Synuclein misfolding propagates prion-like across connected neurons.

**Braak staging of PD**: α-Synuclein pathology begins in:
1. Dorsal motor nucleus of vagus and olfactory bulb (explaining prodromal hyposmia and constipation)
2. Ascending brainstem (locus coeruleus, raphe nuclei)
3. Substantia nigra (motor symptoms emerge)
4. Mesocortex (limbic — cognitive/emotional symptoms)
5–6. Neocortex (dementia)

This staging supports the dual-hit hypothesis: pathology may originate in the gut (vagal route) and/or olfactory mucosa, propagating centrally.

### Motor Symptoms (Cardinal Features)

- **Bradykinesia**: Slowness of movement; key diagnostic feature
- **Resting tremor** (4–6 Hz "pill-rolling"): Due to oscillatory activity in basal ganglia-thalamocortical circuits
- **Rigidity**: Increased resistance to passive movement ("lead-pipe" or "cogwheel" with superimposed tremor)
- **Postural instability**: Late feature; falls

**Basal ganglia model**: Dopamine loss → reduced D1 (direct pathway) stimulation and reduced D2 (indirect pathway) inhibition → net increased GPi output → excessive thalamic inhibition → reduced cortical motor activation → bradykinesia/rigidity.

### Non-Motor Symptoms

Often more disabling than motor symptoms; reflect widespread neurodegeneration beyond SNc:
- **REM sleep behavior disorder** (brainstem; often prodromal by 10+ years)
- **Hyposmia/anosmia** (olfactory bulb; prodromal)
- **Constipation** (enteric nervous system; prodromal)
- **Depression and anxiety** (raphe nuclei, LC degeneration)
- **Cognitive impairment → dementia** (cortical Lewy bodies; ~80% develop PD dementia by 20 years)
- **Autonomic dysfunction** (orthostatic hypotension, urinary problems)
- **Pain and fatigue**

### Treatment

- **Levodopa** (L-DOPA): Precursor to dopamine; crosses BBB (dopamine itself cannot); converted to dopamine by DOPA decarboxylase. Given with carbidopa (peripheral DOPA decarboxylase inhibitor to reduce peripheral side effects). Most effective symptomatic treatment but long-term use → motor complications (wearing-off fluctuations, dyskinesias).
- **Dopamine agonists** (pramipexole, ropinirole): Direct D2/D3 receptor stimulation. Side effects: impulse control disorders (~15% — gambling, hypersexuality, compulsive shopping).
- **MAO-B inhibitors** (selegiline, rasagiline): Reduce dopamine degradation.
- **Deep brain stimulation (DBS)**: High-frequency stimulation of subthalamic nucleus (STN) or GPi — effective for motor fluctuations and dyskinesias. Does not alter disease progression.

## 15.4 Other Neurodegenerative Conditions

### Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD)
Third most common dementia in under-65s. Selective degeneration of frontal and/or temporal lobes.
- **Behavioral variant (bvFTD)**: Personality change, disinhibition, apathy, compulsive behaviors, loss of empathy. Atrophy: Medial PFC, orbitofrontal cortex, anterior insula, anterior cingulate.
- **Primary progressive aphasia (PPA)**: Semantic variant (left anterior temporal — loss of word meaning) or nonfluent variant (left inferior frontal — effortful, agrammatic speech).
- Pathology: Tau (Pick bodies — 3R tau), TDP-43, or FUS protein inclusions. Genetic: ~40% familial (MAPT, GRN, C9orf72 mutations).

### Huntington's Disease (HD)
Autosomal dominant; CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in **huntingtin gene** (HTT, chromosome 4). Normal: <36 repeats; disease: ≥40 repeats (longer repeats → earlier onset). Mutant huntingtin protein → toxic gain of function → selective vulnerability of **medium spiny neurons** in the striatum (caudate > putamen).
- Symptoms: Chorea (involuntary dance-like movements), progressive cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms (depression, irritability), eventual akinetic-rigid state.
- Indirect pathway MSNs (D2, enkephalin) degenerate first → chorea (loss of movement suppression); later direct pathway MSNs (D1, substance P) → rigidity.

### Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)
Progressive degeneration of both **upper motor neurons** (cortex) and **lower motor neurons** (brainstem, spinal cord) → muscle weakness, atrophy, spasticity, fasciculations → respiratory failure (median survival 3–5 years). ~10% familial; mutations in SOD1, C9orf72, TARDBP (TDP-43), FUS. TDP-43 pathology shared with ~50% of FTD cases (ALS-FTD spectrum).

### Lewy Body Dementia (DLB)
Cortical Lewy bodies (α-synuclein) → cognitive fluctuations, visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, REM sleep behavior disorder. Distinguished from PD dementia primarily by timing: DLB = cognitive symptoms within 1 year of motor symptoms (or cognitive first); PDD = cognitive symptoms developing years after established PD.

### Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
Autoimmune demyelinating disease of CNS. T-cells and antibodies attack oligodendrocytes and myelin → inflammation, demyelination, and axonal damage → relapsing-remitting or progressive neurological deficits (optic neuritis, sensory/motor symptoms, fatigue, cognitive impairment). Lesions visible as white matter hyperintensities on MRI. Treatment: Disease-modifying therapies (interferons, natalizumab, ocrelizumab) reduce relapse rate and slow progression.

## 15.5 Principles of Neurodegeneration

Several common themes emerge across neurodegenerative diseases:

1. **Protein misfolding and aggregation**: Aβ/tau (AD), α-synuclein (PD/DLB), huntingtin (HD), TDP-43 (ALS/FTD), prion protein (CJD). Many propagate prion-like across connected neural circuits.
2. **Selective vulnerability**: Specific neuronal populations are preferentially affected in each disease despite widespread protein expression — likely due to intrinsic cellular properties (metabolic demands, calcium handling, receptor expression).
3. **Neuroinflammation**: Activated microglia and astrocytes amplify damage in all neurodegenerative diseases.
4. **Mitochondrial dysfunction**: Impaired energy production and increased oxidative stress.
5. **Impaired proteostasis**: Ubiquitin-proteasome and autophagy-lysosome systems become overwhelmed.
6. **Excitotoxicity**: Glutamate-mediated damage, particularly in ALS and AD.
7. **Long prodromal phases**: Pathology begins years to decades before clinical symptoms, opening a window for early intervention if biomarkers can be detected.
