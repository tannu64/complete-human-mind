# Chapter 9: Emotion and Motivation

## 9.1 Theories of Emotion

Emotion encompasses subjective experience (feelings), physiological responses (autonomic, endocrine), behavioral expression (facial, postural, vocal), and cognitive appraisal. Major theoretical frameworks:

**James-Lange Theory (1884)**: Bodily responses precede and cause emotional experience. Stimulus → physiological response → perception of response = emotion. Modern support: facial feedback hypothesis (attenuated expressions reduce emotional intensity); interoceptive processing in insula correlates with emotional awareness.

**Cannon-Bard Theory (1927)**: Emotional experience and physiological responses occur simultaneously and independently, both triggered by the thalamus. Modern support: emotional experience can occur without peripheral feedback (e.g., spinal cord injury patients still report emotions, though intensity may be somewhat reduced).

**Schachter-Singer Two-Factor Theory (1962)**: Emotion = physiological arousal + cognitive label. Arousal is nonspecific; the cognitive interpretation of the context determines which emotion is experienced. Explains misattribution effects.

**Constructionist Theory (Barrett, 2017)**: Emotions are not biologically basic categories with dedicated neural circuits. Instead, the brain constructs emotional experiences from domain-general processes: interoception (core affect — valence + arousal), conceptual categorization (learned emotion categories), and language. Supported by meta-analyses showing no consistent one-to-one mapping between emotions and brain regions.

**Appraisal Theories** (Lazarus, Scherer): Emotions arise from cognitive evaluation of events along dimensions (goal relevance, goal congruence, agency, coping potential). Different appraisal patterns produce different emotions.

## 9.2 The Amygdala: Emotional Processing Hub

The amygdala is an almond-shaped nuclear complex in the medial temporal lobe, central to emotional processing (see also Chapter 5 for fear circuits).

### Nuclei and Connectivity

**Basolateral complex (BLA = lateral + basal nuclei)**:
- Receives processed sensory input from all modalities (visual, auditory, somatosensory, olfactory, gustatory cortices) + hippocampal contextual information
- Assigns emotional valence (positive and negative — not exclusively fear) to stimuli
- Contains distinct neuronal populations for aversive vs. appetitive associations
- Projects to PFC (emotional influence on cognition), hippocampus (emotional modulation of memory), striatum (emotional influence on action), and CeA

**Central nucleus (CeA)**:
- Primary output of the amygdala
- Organized into lateral (CeL) and medial (CeM) divisions
- CeL contains inhibitory microcircuits that gate CeA output — including PKCδ+ (fear-off) and SOM+ (fear-on) neurons
- CeM projects to: hypothalamus (autonomic responses), PAG (freezing/flight), brainstem nuclei (startle, respiratory, cardiovascular responses), LC (norepinephrine release)

**Extended amygdala**: The bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST) mediates sustained anxiety-like responses (to diffuse/unpredictable threats), in contrast to the CeA which mediates phasic fear responses (to discrete threats). The BNST-CeA distinction maps onto the clinical distinction between anxiety (sustained apprehension) and fear (acute threat response).

### Beyond Fear: Positive Valence

The amygdala is not exclusively a "fear center." The BLA contains neurons that respond to rewards and appetitive stimuli, project to nucleus accumbens, and are necessary for reward-seeking behavior. Amygdala lesions impair not only fear conditioning but also reward devaluation, incentive learning, and social cognition. The amygdala is better conceptualized as a **relevance detector** — processing any biologically or personally significant stimulus.

## 9.3 Prefrontal Cortex and Emotional Regulation

The PFC exerts top-down control over emotional responses through projections to the amygdala, striatum, and hypothalamus:

**Ventromedial PFC (vmPFC / medial orbitofrontal cortex)**:
- Integrates emotional value signals with contextual information
- Essential for fear extinction — vmPFC activation suppresses amygdala CeA output via intercalated cell masses
- Damage: impaired decision-making (Iowa Gambling Task), poor emotion regulation, but preserved IQ; Phineas Gage case
- Somatic marker hypothesis (Damasio): vmPFC stores associations between situations and the bodily/emotional states they previously evoked; these "somatic markers" guide rapid intuitive decision-making

**Dorsolateral PFC (dlPFC)**:
- Cognitive reappraisal of emotions — reinterpreting the meaning of emotional stimuli to change their impact
- dlPFC activation → modulation of amygdala via vmPFC relay
- Working memory and executive function interface with emotion regulation

**Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)**:
- **Dorsal ACC (dACC)**: Conflict monitoring, error detection, cognitive control of emotion, pain affect (the "suffering" component of pain — dissociable from sensory pain in S1)
- **Subgenual ACC (sgACC, BA25)**: Implicated in depression (hyperactivity in MDD); target for deep brain stimulation in treatment-resistant depression
- **Pregenual ACC**: Autonomic regulation, emotional valuation

### Emotion Regulation Strategies

- **Reappraisal** (cognitive change): Reinterpreting the emotional significance of a stimulus. Engages dlPFC and vlPFC → modulates amygdala. Most effective strategy; predicts better psychological health.
- **Suppression** (response modulation): Inhibiting emotional expression without changing the underlying experience. Reduces behavioral output but increases sympathetic nervous system activity and amygdala activation. Less effective; associated with poorer social outcomes.
- **Distraction** (attentional deployment): Directing attention away from the emotional stimulus. Rapidly effective but doesn't change the underlying emotional representation.
- **Extinction learning**: Repeated exposure to a fear-conditioned stimulus without the aversive outcome → vmPFC-mediated suppression of amygdala (basis of exposure therapy for anxiety disorders; see Chapter 5).

## 9.4 Insula: Interoception and Emotion

The insular cortex, buried within the lateral sulcus, is a critical hub for subjective feeling states:

**Posterior insula**: Receives primary interoceptive input — visceral sensations, pain, temperature, itch, sensual touch. Maps body state.

**Mid-insula**: Integrates interoceptive signals with contextual and emotional information.

**Anterior insula (AI)**: Generates a subjective representation of the body's current state — the "material me" (Craig, 2009). The right AI in particular integrates interoception, emotion, time perception, and self-awareness into a unified moment-by-moment sense of embodied selfhood. AI activation correlates with:
- Subjective feeling states across all emotional categories
- Empathy (including pain empathy — "I feel your pain" via shared interoceptive representations)
- Disgust (gustatory, moral, and interpersonal)
- Uncertainty and risk prediction
- Craving and addiction (anticipation of drug effects)
- Social emotions: guilt, embarrassment, trust

## 9.5 The Hypothalamus and Basic Drives

The hypothalamus (~4 g) is the master regulator of homeostatic drives — converting internal state signals into motivated behavior:

### Hunger and Feeding

**Arcuate nucleus (ARC)**: Contains two opposing populations:
- **AgRP/NPY neurons** (orexigenic): Stimulated by ghrelin (from stomach), low glucose, low leptin → drive hunger and food seeking. Optogenetic activation → immediate voracious eating even in sated animals.
- **POMC/CART neurons** (anorexigenic): Stimulated by leptin (from adipose tissue), insulin, PYY (from gut) → suppress appetite. POMC cleavage → α-MSH → MC4R activation → satiety signaling.

**Lateral hypothalamus (LH)**: Contains orexin/hypocretin neurons and MCH neurons that promote feeding and arousal. LH receives AgRP neuron input and projects to VTA (linking hunger to reward-seeking behavior), PAG (foraging), and cortex.

**Ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH)**: "Satiety center" — VMH lesions → hyperphagia and obesity. Also contains SF1 neurons mediating defensive aggression.

**Integration**: Circulating hormones (leptin, ghrelin, insulin, GLP-1, CCK) cross the BBB via arcuate nucleus median eminence (fenestrated capillaries) or signal via vagal afferents → NTS → hypothalamus. The gut-brain axis also includes microbiome-derived signals (short-chain fatty acids, neurotransmitter precursors) influencing appetite and mood (see Chapter 12).

### Thermoregulation

**Preoptic area (POA)**: Contains warm-sensitive neurons that detect core temperature rises → activate heat dissipation (vasodilation, sweating). Cold-sensitive neurons in the POA and dorsomedial hypothalamus → activate heat conservation/production (vasoconstriction, shivering via somatic motor output, non-shivering thermogenesis via brown fat sympathetic activation). Fever: IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α → prostaglandin E2 production in brain vasculature → shifts POA set point upward.

### Thirst

**Subfornical organ (SFO) and organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis (OVLT)**: Circumventricular organs (outside BBB) that detect blood osmolality and angiotensin II levels → project to **median preoptic nucleus** → drive water-seeking behavior and vasopressin release. Recent optogenetic studies show these neurons generate a predictive thirst signal — they are inhibited by water intake within seconds, well before osmolality changes (gut/oropharyngeal anticipatory signals).

### Sexual Behavior

**Medial preoptic area (MPOA)**: Essential for male sexual behavior in all vertebrates studied. Contains aromatase (converts testosterone → estradiol locally) and androgen/estrogen receptors. Receives input from medial amygdala (pheromonal signals in rodents).

**Ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH)**: Mediates lordosis (female sexual receptivity) in rodents; regulated by estrogen and progesterone.

**Human sexual response** is far more cortically mediated than in rodents, involving complex interactions between hypothalamus, amygdala, insula, ACC, orbitofrontal cortex, and reward circuitry.

## 9.6 The Stress Response

### Acute Stress: Fight-or-Flight

**Sympathetic-adrenal-medullary (SAM) axis** (seconds):
Threat perception (amygdala) → hypothalamus → brainstem sympathetic nuclei → preganglionic neurons → sympathetic chain ganglia → postganglionic neurons (norepinephrine) + adrenal medulla (epinephrine release into blood).

Effects: Increased heart rate, blood pressure, bronchodilation, glucose mobilization, pupil dilation, blood flow redistribution to muscles, inhibited digestion, piloerection.

### Sustained Stress: HPA Axis

**Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis** (minutes to hours):

1. **Hypothalamic PVN** releases **CRH** (and AVP) → median eminence → hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal vessels
2. **Anterior pituitary corticotrophs**: CRH binds CRH-R1 → POMC cleavage → **ACTH** release into systemic circulation
3. **Adrenal cortex (zona fasciculata)**: ACTH binds MC2R → **cortisol** synthesis and release

**Cortisol effects on brain**:
- **Hippocampus** (high glucocorticoid receptor [GR] density): Acute moderate cortisol → enhanced memory consolidation. Chronic elevated cortisol → dendritic retraction in CA3, reduced neurogenesis in DG, hippocampal volume reduction, impaired memory.
- **Amygdala**: Chronic stress → dendritic hypertrophy in BLA → increased anxiety and fear reactivity
- **PFC**: Chronic stress → dendritic retraction in medial PFC → impaired working memory and executive function
- **Negative feedback**: Cortisol binds GR in hippocampus, PVN, and pituitary → suppresses CRH and ACTH release. Chronic stress can impair this feedback → sustained HPA activation.

**Allostatic load**: Cumulative wear-and-tear from chronic stress and repeated activation of stress systems → increased risk of cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, immune suppression, depression, cognitive decline, and accelerated aging (shortened telomeres).

### Stress Resilience

Resilience is not merely the absence of pathology but an active neurobiological process:
- **DHEA and neuropeptide Y**: Released during stress; buffer against cortisol's negative effects
- **Medial PFC function**: Maintained top-down regulation of amygdala
- **Hippocampal neurogenesis**: Exercise, social support, and cognitive reappraisal protect neurogenesis from stress-induced suppression
- **Vagal tone**: Higher resting vagal tone (parasympathetic) predicts better stress recovery and emotional regulation

## 9.7 The Autonomic Nervous System

The ANS regulates visceral functions and is intimately linked to emotional states:

### Sympathetic Division ("Fight-or-Flight")
- **Origin**: Intermediolateral cell column (T1–L2 spinal cord)
- **Ganglia**: Paravertebral chain ganglia and prevertebral ganglia
- **Neurotransmitters**: Preganglionic — ACh (nicotinic); postganglionic — norepinephrine (α and β adrenergic receptors); exception: sweat glands (ACh, muscarinic)
- **Effects**: Cardiac acceleration, bronchodilation, vasoconstriction (visceral), vasodilation (skeletal muscle), pupil dilation, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, inhibited digestion, inhibited urination

### Parasympathetic Division ("Rest-and-Digest")
- **Origin**: Brainstem nuclei (cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X) and sacral spinal cord (S2–S4)
- **Ganglia**: Near or within target organs
- **Neurotransmitters**: Preganglionic — ACh (nicotinic); postganglionic — ACh (muscarinic)
- **Vagus nerve (X)**: The primary parasympathetic nerve; innervates heart (slowing), lungs (bronchoconstriction), GI tract (increased motility and secretion), and many abdominal organs. ~80% of vagal fibers are afferent (sensory — gut→brain information flow), making it a major conduit for the gut-brain axis.

### Polyvagal Theory (Porges, 1994)

Proposes three hierarchical autonomic states mediating social behavior:
1. **Ventral vagal complex** (myelinated vagus): Social engagement system — facial expression, vocalization, listening; promotes calm physiological state
2. **Sympathetic system**: Mobilization — fight/flight
3. **Dorsal vagal complex** (unmyelinated vagus): Immobilization — freeze/shutdown, dissociation (extreme threat)

**Note**: Polyvagal theory remains influential in clinical psychology (trauma therapy) but several of its neuroanatomical claims (particularly the evolutionary phylogenetic hierarchy and the strict separation of myelinated/unmyelinated vagal functions) have been criticized as oversimplified or inconsistent with comparative neuroanatomy evidence. The general principle that autonomic state shapes social behavior, however, has broad support.

## 9.8 Social Neuroscience

### Theory of Mind / Mentalizing

The ability to attribute mental states (beliefs, intentions, desires) to others. Neural basis:
- **Medial PFC** (particularly dorsomedial): Self/other distinction, trait inferences
- **Temporoparietal junction (TPJ)**: Perspective-taking, belief attribution (especially right TPJ for reorienting attention to social cues)
- **Posterior superior temporal sulcus (pSTS)**: Perceiving biological motion and social intent
- **Temporal poles**: Social/emotional conceptual knowledge
- These areas overlap significantly with the default mode network.

### Mirror Neuron System

Originally discovered in macaque premotor cortex (area F5): neurons that fire both during action execution and observation of the same action performed by another. Putative mirror neuron regions in humans include premotor cortex, inferior parietal lobule, and superior temporal sulcus.

Proposed functions: Action understanding, imitation, empathy, language evolution. However, the strong claims (mirror neuron dysfunction causes autism; mirror neurons are the basis of empathy) remain controversial and are not well-supported by evidence. The mirror system likely contributes to but does not solely underlie social cognition.

### Empathy

- **Affective empathy** (emotional resonance): Shared neural representations — observing someone in pain activates the observer's anterior insula and ACC (the affective but not sensory components of the pain matrix). Regulated by contextual factors (perceived fairness, in-group/out-group status).
- **Cognitive empathy** (perspective-taking): Mentalizing network (mPFC, TPJ) — understanding another's viewpoint without necessarily sharing their feeling.
- These are dissociable: Psychopathy involves intact cognitive empathy but reduced affective empathy; autism spectrum conditions may involve reduced cognitive empathy (mentalizing difficulty) with preserved or heightened affective empathy.
