# Chapter 12: Neuroendocrine and Neuroimmune Systems

## 12.1 The Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)

The BBB is a selective permeability barrier that separates circulating blood from the brain extracellular fluid, maintaining a tightly controlled chemical environment essential for neural function.

### Structure

The BBB is formed by three components:
1. **Brain endothelial cells**: Connected by **tight junctions** (claudin-5, occludin, ZO-1 proteins) that create a paracellular seal 50–100× tighter than peripheral endothelium. No fenestrations. Very low pinocytosis.
2. **Pericytes**: Embedded in the endothelial basement membrane; regulate BBB integrity, cerebral blood flow, and angiogenesis. Pericyte deficiency → BBB breakdown → neuroinflammation.
3. **Astrocyte endfeet**: Cover ~99% of the capillary surface; induce and maintain BBB properties via secretion of Sonic hedgehog, angiopoietin-1, and other factors.

### Transport Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Examples | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Passive diffusion (lipophilic, <400 Da) | O₂, CO₂, ethanol, nicotine, most anesthetics | Bidirectional |
| Carrier-mediated transport | GLUT1 (glucose), LAT1 (amino acids), MCT1 (lactate) | Typically blood → brain |
| Receptor-mediated transcytosis | Transferrin receptor (iron), LRP1 (ApoE, Aβ clearance), insulin receptor | Blood → brain |
| Efflux transporters | P-glycoprotein (P-gp), BCRP, MRPs | Brain → blood (pumps out lipophilic drugs) |
| Ion transporters | Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, ion channels | Maintains ionic homeostasis |

**BBB-absent regions (circumventricular organs)**: Area postrema (chemoreceptor trigger zone — detects blood-borne toxins → emesis), median eminence (hypothalamic-pituitary hormone exchange), subfornical organ and OVLT (osmolality sensing, angiotensin II detection), pineal gland (melatonin secretion), posterior pituitary (hormone release).

**BBB disruption** occurs in: stroke, traumatic brain injury, multiple sclerosis, brain tumors, meningitis, Alzheimer's disease (early pathological event), and chronic hypertension. Disruption allows entry of plasma proteins, immune cells, and toxins → neuroinflammation and neuronal damage.

## 12.2 The Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axes

The hypothalamus controls the endocrine system through the pituitary gland via two pathways:

### Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis) — Hormonal Control

Hypothalamic neurons release **releasing/inhibiting hormones** into the **hypophyseal portal system** (median eminence capillaries → portal veins → anterior pituitary capillaries):

| Hypothalamic Hormone | Anterior Pituitary Hormone | Target & Effect |
|---|---|---|
| CRH (+ AVP) | ACTH | Adrenal cortex → cortisol (HPA axis) |
| GnRH | FSH, LH | Gonads → sex steroids; gametogenesis (HPG axis) |
| TRH | TSH | Thyroid → T3/T4 (HPT axis) |
| GHRH / Somatostatin | GH | Liver → IGF-1; bone/muscle growth |
| Dopamine (inhibitory) | Prolactin | Breast → lactation; reproductive function |
| CRH + dopamine modulation | POMC → ACTH + β-endorphin, α-MSH | Stress, pain, pigmentation |

**Negative feedback**: Each axis employs feedback loops — target hormones (cortisol, T3/T4, sex steroids) act on hypothalamus and pituitary to suppress releasing hormone secretion, maintaining homeostasis.

### Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis) — Direct Neural Secretion

Magnocellular neurons in PVN and supraoptic nucleus synthesize hormones in their cell bodies and transport them down axons to the posterior pituitary for direct release into blood:

- **Oxytocin**: Uterine contraction (labor), milk letdown reflex, social bonding, trust, maternal behavior. CNS effects via dendritic release: reduces amygdala reactivity, promotes social approach, enhances face recognition, and modulates stress response. Intranasal oxytocin studies show complex and context-dependent effects — it enhances in-group bonding but may increase out-group hostility.

- **Vasopressin (ADH)**: Water reabsorption in kidney collecting ducts (V2 receptors); vasoconstriction (V1a); CNS effects: social recognition memory, aggression, pair bonding (V1a in ventral pallidum — prairie vole model), circadian regulation, stress response modulation.

## 12.3 Thyroid Hormones and the Brain

**Thyroid hormones** (T3, T4) are essential for brain development and adult brain function:

**Development**: Maternal T4 crosses the placenta (especially critical in first trimester before fetal thyroid is functional). T4 is converted to active T3 by type 2 deiodinase (D2) in astrocytes, then delivered to neurons. T3 regulates:
- Neuronal migration (Reelin expression)
- Dendritic and axonal growth
- Synaptogenesis
- Myelination (oligodendrocyte maturation)
- Purkinje cell development in cerebellum

**Congenital hypothyroidism** (untreated): Severe intellectual disability (cretinism). Detected by neonatal screening; treated with levothyroxine.

**Adult brain**: Thyroid hormones modulate neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, norepinephrine), adult hippocampal neurogenesis, and mood. Hypothyroidism → depression, cognitive slowing, memory impairment. Hyperthyroidism → anxiety, irritability, tremor.

## 12.4 Sex Hormones and the Brain

### Organizational vs. Activational Effects

**Organizational effects** (prenatal/perinatal): Sex hormones (primarily testosterone and its metabolite estradiol) permanently organize brain circuits during development:
- **Sexually dimorphic nucleus of the preoptic area (SDN-POA)**: 2.5× larger in males (testosterone → aromatization to estradiol → promotes cell survival in this region)
- **Bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST)**: Larger in males; involved in stress/anxiety
- **Corpus callosum**: Some studies report shape differences; functional significance debated
- Testosterone exposure during critical periods influences spatial cognition, play behavior, aggression, and sexual orientation (according to some organizational hormone theories — the strength of this evidence varies)

**Activational effects** (puberty and adulthood): Circulating sex hormones modulate brain function reversibly:
- **Estradiol** (via ERα, ERβ, and rapid membrane receptors): Enhances hippocampal synaptic plasticity and neurogenesis; increases spine density in CA1; neuroprotective (antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, mitochondrial support). Menstrual cycle variations in estradiol correlate with changes in hippocampal volume (~2%), PFC connectivity, and emotional reactivity. Post-menopausal estrogen decline may contribute to increased AD risk in women.
- **Progesterone**: Metabolized to allopregnanolone — a potent positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A receptors → anxiolytic and sedative effects. Fluctuations in progesterone/allopregnanolone contribute to premenstrual mood symptoms and postpartum depression. Brexanolone (synthetic allopregnanolone) is FDA-approved for postpartum depression.
- **Testosterone**: Modulates amygdala reactivity to threat, dominance behavior, risk-taking, and aggression (though the relationship is bidirectional — social context also modulates testosterone levels).

## 12.5 Neuroinflammation

### Microglia: Brain's Immune Sentinels

(See Chapter 2 for microglial cell biology.)

**Microglial activation states**: The traditional M1 (pro-inflammatory) / M2 (anti-inflammatory) dichotomy is now recognized as an oversimplification. Microglia adopt a spectrum of activation states depending on the nature, duration, and context of the stimulus.

**Acute neuroinflammation** (protective): Response to infection or injury. Microglia and astrocytes release cytokines, clear debris, and form glial scars to contain damage.

**Chronic neuroinflammation** (detrimental): Sustained microglial activation → persistent release of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, reactive oxygen species, nitric oxide → neuronal damage, synaptic dysfunction, impaired neurogenesis. Implicated in:
- **Alzheimer's disease**: Microglia cluster around amyloid plaques; TREM2 risk gene affects microglial phagocytic function; complement-mediated synaptic stripping
- **Parkinson's disease**: Microglial activation in substantia nigra; α-synuclein aggregates activate innate immunity
- **Depression**: Elevated inflammatory cytokines in ~30% of MDD patients ("inflammatory subtype"); IL-1β and TNF-α → reduced serotonin synthesis (IDO enzyme shunts tryptophan → kynurenine instead of serotonin); sickness behavior phenomenology overlaps with depression
- **Traumatic brain injury**: Chronic microglial activation persists years after injury → progressive neurodegeneration
- **Aging** ("inflammaging"): Age-related increase in baseline inflammatory tone → priming microglia for exaggerated response

### Cytokine Signaling in the Brain

Peripheral cytokines communicate with the brain via:
1. **Circumventricular organs** (no BBB)
2. **Vagal afferents**: Detect peripheral inflammation → activate NTS → central cytokine response
3. **Active transport** across BBB (specific cytokine transporters)
4. **BBB endothelial activation**: Peripheral cytokines induce endothelial cells to produce prostaglandins and NO that signal centrally

Central cytokine effects: fever (PGE2 in preoptic area), sickness behavior (lethargy, anorexia, social withdrawal — adaptive response to redirect energy toward immune function), HPA axis activation, altered neurotransmission.

## 12.6 The Gut-Brain Axis

The bidirectional communication system between the gastrointestinal tract and the brain, mediated by neural, hormonal, immune, and microbial pathways.

### Communication Routes

1. **Vagus nerve**: The primary neural highway. ~80% of vagal fibers are afferent (gut → brain). Gut enterochromaffin cells detect luminal contents and release serotonin (5-HT; gut produces ~95% of body's serotonin) → activate vagal afferents → NTS → hypothalamus, amygdala, cortex. Vagotomy reduces the anxiolytic effect of probiotics in animal models.

2. **Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis**: Stress (via cortisol) alters gut permeability, motility, and microbiome composition; gut-derived signals modulate HPA axis reactivity.

3. **Immune pathway**: Gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT, ~70% of immune cells) → cytokine production → crosses BBB → neuroinflammation or anti-inflammatory signaling.

4. **Microbial metabolites**: Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs — butyrate, propionate, acetate from fiber fermentation), tryptophan metabolites, GABA, dopamine precursors → directly or indirectly influence brain function.

### The Microbiome and Brain Function

The human gut harbors ~3.8 × 10¹³ microorganisms (~1:1 ratio with human cells; ~150× more genes than the human genome). Key findings (predominantly animal models with growing human evidence):

- **Germ-free mice**: Exaggerated HPA stress response, altered anxiety behavior, impaired social behavior, reduced BDNF in hippocampus, disrupted BBB integrity — all partially reversible with microbial colonization during a critical window.
- **Specific microbial effects**: Lactobacillus rhamnosus → reduced anxiety (via vagal signaling → altered GABA receptor expression). Bifidobacterium longum → reduced stress reactivity. Bacteroides fragilis → improved social behavior in autism model mice (polysaccharide A → immune modulation).
- **Human studies**: Altered microbiome composition reported in depression, anxiety, autism spectrum disorder, Parkinson's disease, and Alzheimer's disease (though causality is difficult to establish). Probiotic supplementation shows modest but significant effects on anxiety and depression symptoms in meta-analyses.
- **Tryptophan metabolism**: Gut microbiota influence tryptophan availability for serotonin synthesis (via bacterial tryptophan metabolism and IDO pathway modulation).

## 12.7 Circadian Rhythms

### The Master Clock: Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN)

The SCN of the hypothalamus (~20,000 neurons in humans) generates the master circadian rhythm — an endogenous ~24.2 hour oscillation that persists even without external time cues.

**Molecular clock mechanism** (transcription-translation feedback loop):
1. **CLOCK** and **BMAL1** transcription factors heterodimerize → activate transcription of Period (Per1, Per2, Per3) and Cryptochrome (Cry1, Cry2) genes
2. PER and CRY proteins accumulate in cytoplasm → heterodimerize → translocate to nucleus → inhibit CLOCK/BMAL1 activity (negative feedback)
3. PER/CRY degradation (CK1ε phosphorylation → ubiquitination) resets the cycle (~24 h period)
4. Secondary loops (REV-ERBα/RORα → rhythmic BMAL1 transcription) add stability

**Entrainment (synchronization to environment)**: Light is the primary zeitgeber (time-giver). Retinal ipRGCs (melanopsin) → retinohypothalamic tract → SCN. Light exposure in the morning advances the clock; evening light delays it.

### SCN Outputs

The SCN synchronizes peripheral clocks throughout the brain and body via:
- **Neural projections**: To PVN (→ sympathetic nervous system → pineal gland melatonin), dorsomedial hypothalamus (→ feeding/activity rhythms), VLPO (→ sleep-wake cycle), lateral hypothalamus (→ orexin/hypocretin)
- **Humoral signals**: Transforming growth factor α (TGFα), prokineticin 2 (PK2), cardiotrophin-like cytokine
- **Melatonin**: Synthesized in the pineal gland during darkness (tryptophan → serotonin → N-acetylserotonin → melatonin by AANAT and ASMT enzymes). Melatonin acts on MT1 and MT2 receptors in the SCN (negative feedback) and throughout the body to synchronize peripheral oscillators, modulate immune function, and promote sleep onset.

### Circadian Regulation of Brain Function

Nearly every aspect of brain function shows circadian variation:
- **Cognition**: Working memory and executive function peak in late morning; alertness dips at ~2–4 PM (post-lunch dip); complex reasoning peaks in late afternoon
- **Mood**: Depression symptoms often worst in early morning (diurnal variation); disrupted circadian rhythms are a core feature of major depressive disorder and bipolar disorder
- **Memory**: Hippocampal LTP shows circadian variation (stronger during active phase); sleep-dependent memory consolidation is gated by circadian phase
- **Neurotransmitter levels**: Cortical serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine show diurnal fluctuations
- **BBB permeability**: Circadian variation in efflux transporter activity → drug timing matters (chronopharmacology)

### Chronotype and Disease

**Chronotype** (morning lark vs. evening owl) is ~50% heritable, influenced by PER2, PER3, CRY1, and CLOCK gene variants. Misalignment between internal clock and social schedule ("social jetlag") is associated with increased risk of metabolic syndrome, depression, and cognitive impairment. Shift work disrupts circadian rhythms and is classified as a probable carcinogen (WHO/IARC); it increases risk of cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, and possibly neurodegenerative disease.
