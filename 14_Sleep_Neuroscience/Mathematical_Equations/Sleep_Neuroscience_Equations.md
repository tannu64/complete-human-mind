# Chapter 14: Sleep Neuroscience — Mathematical Equations

## Section 1: Sleep Architecture and EEG Spectral Properties

### 1.1 Delta Power and Sleep Depth

The dominant brain oscillation during NREM stage N3 (deep/slow-wave sleep) is the delta rhythm (0.5–4 Hz). Delta power (measured in μV², integrated from 0.5–4 Hz in the EEG spectrum) increases with sleep pressure and correlates with sleep depth and cognitive recovery.

$$\text{Delta power} = A \times (1 - e^{-\lambda t}) \times e^{-\mu \cdot t_{since\_N3\_onset}}$$

or more simply, delta power increases with time asleep and decreases as the night progresses (reflecting dissipation of sleep pressure):

$$P_{\Delta}(t) = P_{max} \times \frac{1 - e^{-\alpha t}}{1 + e^{-\beta(T - t)}}$$

where $T$ is the total sleep period and $t$ is time elapsed.

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $P_{\Delta}$ | Delta power (0.5–4 Hz; μV²) | 50–200 μV² (varies by electrode) |
| $P_{max}$ | Maximum delta power (well-rested) | ~150–200 μV² |
| $\alpha$ | Time constant for delta power buildup | ~0.05–0.1 min⁻¹ |
| $\beta$ | Decay factor for delta power over night | ~0.01–0.05 min⁻¹ |
| $t$ | Time into sleep period (min) | 0–480 min (8 h) |
| $T$ | Total sleep period | ~480 min (8 h) |

**Explanation**: Delta power peaks early in the night (first NREM deep sleep episodes) and declines progressively as sleep continues, reflecting homeostatic sleep pressure dissipation. Sleep deprivation increases delta power (rebound), indicating higher sleep need. Delta power is one of the most reliable markers of sleep debt and sleep quality.

---

### 1.2 Sleep Spindle Frequency and Learning-Related Power

Sleep spindles (brief bursts at 12–16 Hz lasting 0.5–2 seconds) occur during N2 sleep and correlate with learning capacity and IQ. Spindle power integrates over the 12–16 Hz frequency band:

$$P_{spindle} = P_{baseline}^{spindle} + k_{learning} \times \text{(amount of learning that day)}$$

Spindle frequency is related to cognitive ability (higher frequency = better learning):

$$f_{spindle} = f_{baseline} + m \times IQ_{percentile}$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $P_{spindle}$ | Spindle power (12–16 Hz; μV²) | 5–50 μV² |
| $P_{baseline}^{spindle}$ | Baseline spindle power | ~10–15 μV² |
| $k_{learning}$ | Learning-dependent increase in spindle activity | 0.01–0.1 μV²/(hour of learning) |
| $f_{spindle}$ | Spindle frequency (Hz) | 12–16 Hz |
| $f_{baseline}$ | Baseline spindle frequency | ~13–14 Hz (individual-dependent) |
| $m$ | Slope of IQ-spindle frequency relationship | ~0.02–0.05 Hz per IQ percentile |

**Explanation**: Spindles are generated by the thalamic reticular nucleus and mediate both sleep protection (sensory gating) and memory consolidation. Higher spindle power predicts better learning of motor and declarative tasks. Sleep spindles may reflect the quality of thalamocortical information processing during sleep.

## Section 2: Process S — Homeostatic Sleep Pressure

### 2.1 Adenosine Accumulation During Wakefulness

Adenosine accumulates in the brain as a byproduct of ATP metabolism during wakefulness. The concentration follows approximately first-order exponential accumulation:

$$[Adenosine](t) = [Adenosine]_{max} \times (1 - e^{-k_a \cdot t})$$

where $t$ is time awake, $k_a$ is the accumulation rate constant, and $[Adenosine]_{max}$ is the asymptotic maximum concentration.

Alternatively, with basal adenosine:

$$[Adenosine](t) = [Adenosine]_{basal} + ([Adenosine]_{peak} - [Adenosine]_{basal}) \times (1 - e^{-k_a \cdot t})$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $[Adenosine](t)$ | Adenosine concentration at time $t$ | 0.1–1.0 μM (basal); 1–5 μM (elevated) |
| $[Adenosine]_{basal}$ | Basal adenosine (resting) | ~0.1–0.3 μM |
| $[Adenosine]_{peak}$ | Peak adenosine after prolonged wakefulness | ~1–5 μM |
| $k_a$ | Adenosine accumulation rate constant | ~0.005–0.01 h⁻¹ (half-life ~70–140 h, but locally faster) |
| $t$ | Time awake (hours) | 0–24 h |
| Half-saturation time | Time to reach 50% of peak adenosine | ~70–140 min wakefulness |

**Explanation**: Adenosine accumulates as neurons consume ATP for ion pumps and synaptic transmission. High adenosine levels suppress wake-promoting neurons (via A1 receptors) and promote sleep-promoting neurons in the VLPO (via A2A receptors). This is the molecular basis of homeostatic sleep pressure. Caffeine blocks both A1 and A2A receptors, temporarily masking sleepiness without reducing the actual adenosine burden.

---

### 2.2 Adenosine Clearance During Sleep

During sleep, especially NREM slow-wave sleep, adenosine is rapidly cleared via cellular uptake and metabolism. The clearance follows first-order kinetics:

$$[Adenosine](t) = [Adenosine]_0 \times e^{-k_c \cdot t}$$

where $k_c$ is the clearance rate constant (much faster during sleep than wakefulness).

The half-life of adenosine during sleep:

$$t_{1/2}^{sleep} = \frac{\ln(2)}{k_c} \approx 20–40 \text{ minutes}$$

versus wakefulness:

$$t_{1/2}^{wake} \approx 50–100 \text{ minutes}$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $k_c$ | Adenosine clearance rate constant | ~0.01–0.03 min⁻¹ (sleep); ~0.005–0.01 min⁻¹ (wake) |
| $t_{1/2}$ | Adenosine half-life | 20–40 min (sleep); 50–100 min (wake) |
| $[Adenosine]_0$ | Initial adenosine at sleep onset | 1–5 μM |
| Time to 90% clearance | Time to clear 90% of adenosine | ~60–130 min of sleep |

**Explanation**: Sleep activates adenosine transporters and nucleotidases, clearing adenosine ~2–3× faster than during wakefulness. The exponential decay of adenosine during sleep drives the rapid decline in delta power and sleep pressure as the night progresses. This is why the first sleep cycle contains more deep sleep (higher delta) than subsequent cycles.

---

### 2.3 Adenosine Receptor Signaling: A1 and A2A Sleep Promotion

Adenosine acts on two main receptors to promote sleep:
- **A1 receptors** (inhibitory): Suppress wake-promoting neurons in lateral hypothalamus and basal forebrain
- **A2A receptors** (excitatory): Activate sleep-promoting neurons in VLPO and nucleus accumbens

The combined effect of adenosine on sleep-wake state can be modeled as:

$$\text{Sleep pressure} = f_{A1}([Adenosine]) + f_{A2A}([Adenosine])$$

where each function follows Hill kinetics:

$$f_{A1}([Adenosine]) = \frac{[Adenosine]^{n_{A1}}}{K_{d,A1}^{n_{A1}} + [Adenosine]^{n_{A1}}}$$

$$f_{A2A}([Adenosine]) = \frac{[Adenosine]^{n_{A2A}}}{K_{d,A2A}^{n_{A2A}} + [Adenosine]^{n_{A2A}}}$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $K_{d,A1}$ | Dissociation constant for A1 receptors | ~5–10 μM |
| $K_{d,A2A}$ | Dissociation constant for A2A receptors | ~1–2 μM |
| $n_{A1}, n_{A2A}$ | Hill coefficients (cooperativity) | 1.5–2.0 |
| $[Adenosine]$ | Adenosine concentration | 0.1–5 μM |
| Caffeine IC₅₀ | Caffeine concentration blocking 50% of receptors | ~10–30 μM (in vitro) |

**Explanation**: A2A receptors have lower Kd (higher affinity) than A1, so they are activated at lower adenosine concentrations. Both contribute to sleepiness. Caffeine competitively blocks both, preventing adenosine signaling. However, caffeine does not eliminate adenosine accumulation—sleep debt remains, and "caffeine crash" occurs when the drug wears off (caffeine half-life ~5 hours).

## Section 3: Process C — Circadian Alerting Signal

### 3.1 Circadian Rhythm of SCN Output

The suprachiasmatic nucleus generates a circadian rhythm of alerting that opposes sleep pressure during the day. The circadian signal can be modeled as a sinusoidal oscillation modulated by an envelope:

$$C(t) = C_0 + A_C \times \sin\left(\frac{2\pi(t - \phi_C)}{T_C}\right) \times \left(1 - e^{-\beta_C \cdot (t \bmod 24)}\right)$$

where $C_0$ is baseline, $A_C$ is amplitude, $\phi_C$ is phase, $T_C$ is the circadian period (~24.2 h), and the envelope term adds evening enhancement ("wake maintenance zone" at ~2–3 hours before bedtime).

More simply, the alertness signal follows:

$$\text{Alertness}(t) = A_0 + A_c \times \sin\left(\frac{2\pi t}{24} + \psi\right)$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $C(t)$ | Circadian alerting signal (arbitrary units) | 0–1 (normalized) |
| $C_0$ | Baseline circadian signal | 0.5 (arbitrary) |
| $A_C$ | Amplitude of circadian oscillation | 0.3–0.5 |
| $\phi_C$ | Phase offset (determined by light exposure) | 0–24 h |
| $T_C$ | Circadian period (free-running) | 24.2 h (humans; slightly longer than 24 h) |
| $\psi$ | Phase shift from clock time | −2 to +2 h (chronotype-dependent) |
| Peak alertness time | Time of maximum circadian alerting | ~8 PM (early evening) |
| Circadian nadir | Time of minimum alerting | ~3–4 AM |

**Explanation**: The circadian signal is generated by the SCN and strengthens throughout the day, peaking in the evening before declining at night. This explains the "wake maintenance zone"—the hard-to-sleep period 2–3 hours before usual bedtime, despite high sleep pressure. Jet lag and shift work cause misalignment between this circadian signal and Process S, creating sleep problems.

---

### 3.2 Two-Process Model: Interaction of S and C

The two-process model of Borbély describes sleep-wake timing as the balance between homeostatic sleep pressure (Process S) and circadian alerting (Process C):

$$\text{Sleep occurs when } S(t) > C(t) + \Theta_{threshold}$$

where $\Theta_{threshold}$ is the threshold for sleep initiation.

The combined state variable is:

$$\Delta W(t) = S(t) - C(t)$$

Sleep onset occurs when $\Delta W > \Theta_{sleep}$ and wake onset occurs when $S$ drops below a wake threshold $\Theta_{wake}$ (despite elevated $C$ in morning).

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $S(t)$ | Homeostatic sleep pressure (Process S) | 0–1 (normalized; increases with wakefulness) |
| $C(t)$ | Circadian alerting signal (Process C) | 0–1 (normalized; higher during day) |
| $\Theta_{sleep}$ | Threshold for sleep initiation | ~0.7–0.8 |
| $\Theta_{wake}$ | Threshold for wake initiation | ~0.3–0.4 |
| Sleep duration optimal | Hours of sleep for homeostatic balance | 7–9 h (individual variation) |

**Explanation**: In well-entrained individuals, Process C peaks in evening (low C, favors sleep) when Process S is still elevated. At night, S declines rapidly due to sleep-dependent adenosine clearance, while C remains low. In morning, C rises sharply (morning light advances the clock), forcing wake despite residual S. Misalignment (jet lag) occurs when S and C become uncoupled—sleep pressure is high but circadian alerting prevents sleep, or vice versa. Re-entrainment takes ~1 day per hour of time zone change (roughly following the PRC of the circadian clock).

## Section 4: Sleep-Wake Flip-Flop Switch

### 4.1 Mutual Inhibition Between Sleep and Wake Circuits

The VLPO (sleep-promoting) and wake-promoting nuclei (locus coeruleus, raphe nuclei, tuberomammillary nucleus) mutually inhibit each other, creating a bistable switch. This can be modeled using coupled nonlinear equations:

$$\frac{dV_{VLPO}}{dt} = -V_{VLPO} + f(I_{orexin} - I_{wake})$$

$$\frac{dV_{wake}}{dt} = -V_{wake} + f(I_{ach} + I_{GABA,from\ VLPO})$$

where $f$ is a sigmoidal activation function and $I$ represents synaptic inputs. The mutual inhibition creates hysteresis—once the switch tips from wake to sleep, it remains in sleep until $I_{orexin}$ (or other wake signals) sufficiently increases.

Stability regions:
- **Sleep state stable**: VLPO active (inhibits wake circuits); maintained by adenosine A1/A2A signaling
- **Wake state stable**: Wake nuclei active (inhibit VLPO via GABA/ACh); maintained by orexin and locus coeruleus NA

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $V_{VLPO}$ | VLPO neuronal firing rate (activity) | 0–100 Hz (arbitrary scale) |
| $V_{wake}$ | Wake-promoting nuclei firing rate | 0–100 Hz |
| $I_{orexin}$ | Orexin input to wake nuclei | 0–1 (normalized) |
| $I_{adenosine}$ | Adenosine input to sleep-promoting neurons | 0–1 (normalized) |
| Hysteresis width | Difference in thresholds for sleep→wake vs wake→sleep | ~10–20% of firing rate range |

**Explanation**: This flip-flop switch explains the sharp transitions between sleep and wake (rather than gradual changes) and provides stability against brief disturbances. Orexin neurons stabilize the switch in the wake position by exciting all wake-promoting nuclei while inhibiting VLPO. Loss of orexin (narcolepsy type 1) destabilizes the switch, causing inappropriate transitions between sleep and wake states, cataplexy (sudden loss of muscle tone), and sleep paralysis.

---

### 4.2 Orexin Stabilization of Wake State

Orexin/hypocretin neurons (in lateral hypothalamus) project widely to all wake-promoting nuclei and also strongly inhibit VLPO GABAergic neurons. The stabilizing effect can be modeled as:

$$\text{Wake stability} = \text{Orexin tone} \times \sum_i (\text{wake nucleus}_i \text{ activity})$$

or in terms of the orexin modulation factor:

$$f_{orexin} = \frac{[Orexin]^{n_{orx}}}{K_{d,orx}^{n_{orx}} + [Orexin]^{n_{orx}}}$$

The loss of orexin neurons reduces the "stiffness" of the wake state:

$$\Delta t_{switch} = \Delta t_{normal} \times \frac{1}{1 + f_{orexin}}$$

where $\Delta t_{switch}$ is the time constant for state transitions.

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $[Orexin]$ | Orexin concentration in wake circuits | 0.1–1 nM (varies by brain region) |
| $K_{d,orx}$ | Orexin receptor dissociation constant | ~1–10 nM |
| $n_{orx}$ | Hill coefficient for orexin signaling | 1.5–2.0 |
| $\Delta t_{normal}$ | Normal state transition time constant | ~30–60 s (sharp transitions) |
| $\Delta t_{narcolepsy}$ | Transition time in orexin loss (narcolepsy) | ~5–10 s (rapid, unstable) |

**Explanation**: In narcolepsy type 1, autoimmune destruction of 70–90% of orexin neurons removes this stabilizing signal. The weakened wake state becomes vulnerable to brief intrusions of REM sleep (cataplexy during emotion, hypnagogic hallucinations during sleep-wake transitions). CSF orexin-A levels <110 pg/mL (vs >200 pg/mL normal) is diagnostic.

## Section 5: REM Sleep Circuitry

### 5.1 REM-On vs REM-Off Flip-Flop Switch

REM sleep is controlled by a second bistable switch between REM-on (sublaterodorsal nucleus, SLD) and REM-off (ventrolateral periaqueductal gray, vlPAG) neurons:

$$\text{REM}_{\text{on}} = \frac{f_{REM-on} - f_{REM-off}}{f_{REM-on} + f_{REM-off}}$$

where the numerator and denominator represent the relative firing of REM-on and REM-off populations. When this ratio >0, REM sleep occurs; <0, NREM or wake occurs.

The transition probability between REM and NREM follows:

$$P(\text{REM} \rightarrow \text{Wake/NREM}) = \lambda_{REM-off} \times e^{-t/\tau_{REM}}$$

where $\lambda_{REM-off}$ is the activation rate of REM-off neurons and $\tau_{REM}$ is the REM episode time constant (~30–60 min).

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $f_{REM-on}$ | Firing rate of SLD (REM-on) neurons | 0–100 Hz |
| $f_{REM-off}$ | Firing rate of vlPAG (REM-off) neurons | 0–100 Hz |
| $\tau_{REM}$ | Time constant of REM episode duration | ~30–60 min |
| REM episode duration | Typical duration of REM period | ~5–30 min (increases across night) |
| REM threshold (serotonin, NE suppression) | Brainstem monoamine tone reduction triggers REM | Activation of cholinergic systems (PPT/LDT) |

**Explanation**: The SLD generates REM sleep when monoamine tone (from locus coeruleus and raphe) is low (which naturally occurs during sleep transitions). The vlPAG maintains REM-off during wakefulness (via noradrenergic and serotonergic inhibition) and NREM sleep. Loss of REM atonia circuitry (via degeneration of the SLD → ventromedial medulla pathway) causes REM sleep behavior disorder (acting out dreams), a prodromal symptom of α-synucleinopathy.

---

### 5.2 REM Muscle Atonia via Glycinergic Inhibition

During REM sleep, spinal motor neurons are selectively hyperpolarized via glycinergic and GABAergic inputs from sublaterodorsal nucleus → ventromedial medulla (motor inhibitory neurons). The inhibitory conductance during REM:

$$g_{REM} = g_{baseline} + g_{GABA} + g_{Gly}$$

The resulting postsynaptic potential:

$$V_m = E_{Cl} \times \frac{g_{GABA} + g_{Gly}}{g_L + g_{GABA} + g_{Gly}} + E_{rest} \times \frac{g_L}{g_L + g_{GABA} + g_{Gly}}$$

where $E_{Cl} \approx −70$ mV (chloride reversal potential).

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $g_{GABA}$ | GABAergic conductance | 10–50 nS (picoSiemens) |
| $g_{Gly}$ | Glycinergic conductance | 5–20 nS |
| $g_L$ | Leak conductance | 2–5 nS |
| $V_m$ | Membrane potential during REM atonia | −70 to −85 mV (hyperpolarized) |
| $E_{Cl}$ | Chloride reversal potential | −70 mV |
| Atonia threshold | Conductance required for complete motor inhibition | ~30–50 nS total inhibitory |

**Explanation**: The combined glycinergic and GABAergic inhibition clamps spinal motor neuron membrane potential near the chloride reversal potential (−70 mV), rendering them effectively inexcitable despite descending motor commands from the brain. Loss of REM atonia (due to brainstem pathology) allows physical movement during dreams (REM behavior disorder).

## Section 6: Glymphatic Waste Clearance System

### 6.1 Interstitial Space Expansion During Sleep (AQP4 Dynamics)

The glymphatic system clears brain waste during sleep via CSF-interstitial fluid exchange. During sleep, **interstitial space volume expands ~60%** due to neuronal shrinkage (reduced noradrenergic tone from locus coeruleus). This expansion can be modeled as:

$$V_{ISF}(state) = V_0 + \Delta V_{sleep} \times (1 - e^{-t/\tau_{shrink}})$$

where $V_0$ is baseline interstitial volume (awake), $\Delta V_{sleep}$ is the additional volume during sleep, and $\tau_{shrink}$ is the neuronal shrinkage time constant.

Aquaporin-4 water channel flux (driving CSF-ISF exchange):

$$J_{AQP4} = g_{AQP4} \times (\Psi_{CSF} - \Psi_{ISF})$$

where $\Psi$ is the osmotic potential. Increased interstitial volume reduces diffusion distance and increases convective flow efficiency.

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $V_{ISF}$ | Interstitial fluid volume | 14% of brain volume (awake); 22% (sleep) |
| $\Delta V_{sleep}$ | Expansion during sleep | ~8% of total brain volume |
| $\tau_{shrink}$ | Time constant for neuronal shrinkage | ~5–10 min at sleep onset |
| $g_{AQP4}$ | AQP4 channel conductance | ~0.1–1 pL·s⁻¹·osmol⁻¹ (per cell) |
| Glymphatic clearance efficiency | Waste clearance rate (sleep vs wake) | 1.6–2× higher during sleep |

**Explanation**: The interstitial space expansion during sleep acts like "opening the microscopic highways" of the brain, allowing CSF to more efficiently mix with interstitial fluid and flush out accumulated metabolic waste (amyloid-β, tau, lipofuscin). The mechanism is driven by reduced noradrenergic tone from the locus coeruleus (norepinephrine normally keeps neurons depolarized and inflated). This glymphatic function is **most efficient during slow-wave sleep** (delta waves), explaining the strong link between sleep and neurotoxin clearance.

---

### 6.2 Amyloid-Beta Clearance Rate (Sleep vs Wakefulness)

Amyloid-β (Aβ), the pathological protein in Alzheimer's disease, is cleared from the brain via the glymphatic system at rates proportional to glymphatic efficiency:

$$\text{Clearance rate}_{A\beta} = k_{clr} \times f_{glymphatic}$$

where $f_{glymphatic}$ is a function of sleep state and interstitial volume:

$$f_{glymphatic} = 1 + 0.6 \times [SWS\ present] + 0.2 \times [NREM\ present]$$

In quantitative terms:

$$[A\beta](t) = [A\beta]_0 \times e^{-k_{clr}(state) \times t}$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $k_{clr}^{wake}$ | Aβ clearance rate constant (wakefulness) | ~0.1–0.2 h⁻¹ |
| $k_{clr}^{NREM}$ | Aβ clearance rate constant (NREM) | ~0.2–0.3 h⁻¹ |
| $k_{clr}^{SWS}$ | Aβ clearance rate constant (slow-wave sleep) | ~0.25–0.4 h⁻¹ |
| Aβ half-life (wake) | Time to clear 50% of Aβ | ~3.5–7 h |
| Aβ half-life (sleep) | Time to clear 50% of Aβ | ~1.7–3.5 h |
| [Aβ]_{pathological} | CSF Aβ threshold for Alzheimer's accumulation | >200 pg/mL (elevated) |

**Explanation**: Chronic sleep deprivation or fragmentation (as in sleep apnea, insomnia) impairs Aβ clearance, allowing pathological accumulation. Amyloid-β PET imaging shows higher Aβ burden in poor sleepers. This provides a mechanistic link between sleep disruption and Alzheimer's disease risk (epidemiologically, sleeping <5 h/night is associated with increased dementia risk, partially mediated by impaired Aβ clearance).

## Section 7: Memory Consolidation During Sleep

### 7.1 Three-Way Coordination: Slow Oscillations, Spindles, and Ripples

Memory consolidation during NREM sleep involves coordinated activity among three neural oscillations that occur within precise temporal windows:

1. **Slow oscillations** (~0.75 Hz): Cortical UP states provide temporal windows for...
2. **Sleep spindles** (12–16 Hz): Spindles occur during slow oscillation UP states and provide Ca²⁺ influx windows for LTP, then...
3. **Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples** (~140–200 Hz): Ripples replay memories from hippocampus to cortex during cortical DOWN states

The probability that ripples occur during the optimal phase window is:

$$P_{ripple|optimal} = \frac{\int_0^T \delta(ripple - spindle\ window) \, dt}{T}$$

A quantitative model uses nested oscillations:

$$\text{Consolidation efficiency} = \underbrace{\sin(\omega_{slow} \cdot t)}_{\text{Slow oscillation}} \times \underbrace{\sin(\omega_{spindle} \cdot t + \phi_{ripple})}_{\text{Spindle-ripple phase coupling}}$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $f_{slow}$ | Slow oscillation frequency | ~0.75 Hz |
| $f_{spindle}$ | Sleep spindle frequency | 12–16 Hz |
| $f_{ripple}$ | Hippocampal ripple frequency | 140–200 Hz |
| $\phi_{ripple}$ | Phase offset of ripples relative to spindles | ~0–90° (ripples during late spindle) |
| Consolidation window duration | Time window for optimal ripple-spindle coupling | ~100–500 ms |
| Coordination efficiency | Fraction of ripples occurring in optimal window | 20–40% (higher in well-consolidated memories) |

**Explanation**: The precise coupling of these three oscillations is critical for memory consolidation. When this coordination is disrupted (e.g., by sleep fragmentation in sleep apnea, or by sleep deprivation), memory consolidation is impaired. Single-unit recordings during sleep show that neurons replaying memories during ripples are most active when spindles occur during slow oscillation UP states, maximizing synaptic plasticity.

---

### 7.2 Hippocampal Replay and Memory Reactivation During Ripples

During ripples, neurons in hippocampus that fired together during learning ("place cell" sequences) replay at ~10× speed during sleep. The replay probability is modeled as:

$$P_{replay}(t) = \sum_i \sin(\omega_{ripple} \cdot t + \phi_i) \times w_i$$

where $w_i$ are synaptic weights reflecting experience. The cumulative effect on cortical strengthening:

$$\Delta w_{cortical} \propto \int (\text{ripple activity}) \times (\text{spindle activity}) \, dt$$

A simpler model: the fraction of learned content replayed per night:

$$\text{Consolidation} = \sum_{\text{ripples}} P(\text{memory}, ripple) \times Strength(memory)$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $\omega_{ripple}$ | Ripple oscillation frequency | ~150–200 Hz |
| $\phi_i$ | Phase offset for each memory component | 0–2π |
| $w_i$ | Synaptic weight (strength of memory trace) | 0–1 (normalized) |
| Replay rate during SWS | Number of ripples per minute | 1–5 ripples/min |
| Memories per ripple | Memory sequences replayed per ripple | 1–10 place cell sequences (mice); likely similar in humans |
| Total replay coverage per night | Fraction of day's learning content replayed | 20–80% (more for important/new memories) |

**Explanation**: Replay during ripples transfers hippocampal declarative memories to cortex for long-term storage. Stronger memories (those practiced more or with higher emotional salience) are replayed more frequently. Sleep-deprived individuals show reduced ripple rate and impaired consolidation of new information. This explains why "sleeping on it" improves memory and why sleep deprivation impairs learning and memory formation.

## Section 8: Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis (SHY)

### 8.1 Synaptic Potentiation During Wakefulness

During wakefulness, learning and experience lead to net synaptic potentiation—an increase in synaptic strength throughout cortical networks. The average change in synaptic weight across all synapses:

$$\overline{w}(t) = \overline{w}_0 + \Delta w_{learning}(t) - \Delta w_{noise}(t)$$

where $\Delta w_{learning}$ is synaptic potentiation from learning and $\Delta w_{noise}$ is some stochastic weakening. Over a day of wakefulness:

$$\overline{w}_{wake} = \overline{w}_{baseline} \times (1 + k_w \cdot t_{wake})$$

where $k_w$ is the rate of synaptic potentiation (~few percent per hour of activity).

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $\overline{w}$ | Average synaptic weight (population) | 0–1 (normalized) |
| $\overline{w}_0$ | Baseline synaptic weight at wake | ~0.5 |
| $\Delta w_{learning}$ | Synaptic weight change from learning | +0.01–0.1 per learning episode |
| $k_w$ | Rate of daytime synaptic potentiation | ~0.02–0.05 per hour |
| $t_{wake}$ | Hours of wakefulness | 0–16 h |
| Max synaptic weight (saturation) | Physiological ceiling for synaptic strength | ~0.8–0.9 |

**Explanation**: LTP and learning during the day strengthen synapses. Without a mechanism to reverse this (synaptic downscaling), synapses would eventually saturate, energy demands would skyrocket (each additional AMPA receptor costs ATP), and the signal-to-noise ratio would degrade. The SHY proposes that NREM sleep provides proportional synaptic downscaling to solve this problem.

---

### 8.2 Synaptic Downscaling During NREM Sleep

During NREM slow-wave sleep, synaptic strength is globally downscaled by ~15–20%, proportional to waking potentiation. AMPA receptor phosphorylation (which increases receptor conductance) decreases during sleep:

$$\overline{w}_{sleep} = \overline{w}_{wake} \times (1 - \alpha)$$

where $\alpha$ is the downscaling factor (~0.15–0.25 during a full night's sleep).

The synaptic weight trajectory:

$$w(t) = w_{wake} \times e^{-k_d \cdot t_{sleep}}$$

where $k_d$ is the downscaling rate constant.

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $\alpha$ | Proportional downscaling factor | 0.15–0.25 (15–25% reduction per night) |
| $k_d$ | Downscaling rate constant | ~0.1–0.2 per 8 h sleep |
| $\overline{w}_{sleep}$ | Average synaptic weight after sleep | 0.4–0.45 (after 0.2 reduction from wake) |
| AMPA receptor phosphorylation | Phosphorylation level change | −20–40% during SWS |
| Spine head volume change | Structural correlate of synaptic strength | −5–15% during sleep |
| Time to 50% downscaling | Time for proportional reduction of synapses by half | ~4–5 h of SWS |

**Explanation**: Sleep provides global "reset" of synaptic strength, erasing weak non-memory-related synapses while preserving (or even strengthening) memories tagged for consolidation. This balances the competing demands of: (1) maintaining plasticity for future learning (by preventing synaptic saturation), (2) conserving energy (weaker synapses require less ATP), and (3) improving signal-to-noise (removing weak noise-related potentiation). Sleep-deprived individuals show saturated LTP (cannot form new memories) and elevated metabolic costs.

## Section 9: Sleep and Immune Function

### 9.1 Pro-Inflammatory Cytokine Production During Sleep

NREM sleep enhances pro-inflammatory cytokine production (IL-1β, TNF-α) from microglia and other immune cells, promoting both sleep and immune defense. Cytokine production during sleep:

$$[Cytokine]_{sleep} = [Cytokine]_{wake} \times (1 + k_{immune} \times t_{NREM})$$

or with saturation:

$$[Cytokine]_{sleep} = \frac{C_{max} \times t_{NREM}}{K_{sat} + t_{NREM}}$$

Sleep deprivation suppresses this response, impairing infection control.

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $[IL-1\beta]_{wake}$ | IL-1β during wakefulness | 1–2 pg/mL (baseline) |
| $[IL-1\beta]_{NREM}$ | IL-1β during NREM sleep | 5–20 pg/mL (elevated) |
| $[TNF-\alpha]_{sleep}$ | TNF-α during NREM sleep | 2–5 pg/mL (elevated) |
| $k_{immune}$ | Immune enhancement rate during sleep | 0.1–0.5 per hour NREM |
| $t_{NREM}$ | Duration of NREM sleep (h) | 0–6 h |
| Sleep-immune coupling | Effect on infection resistance | 4.2–5× increased cold susceptibility with <6 h sleep |

**Explanation**: Infection and inflammation trigger sleep (via cytokine signaling to sleep circuits). During sleep, this same cytokine elevation supports immune defense (T cell redistribution, antibody production, NK cell activation). This bidirectional relationship explains why fever-associated sleepiness is adaptive (sleeping supports immune response) and why sleep deprivation impairs immunity (no cytokine-driven enhancement).

## Section 10: Sleep Disorders and Pathology

### 10.1 Orexin Loss and Adenosine Dysregulation in Sleep Disorders

Sleep disorders can involve disruption of either Process S (adenosine homeostasis) or Process C (circadian rhythm) or the flip-flop switches.

**Narcolepsy type 1** (orexin loss):
$$\text{Stability}_{wake} = 1 - \frac{[\text{Orexin}]}{[\text{Orexin}]_{normal}}$$

With 70–90% orexin loss, wake state stability drops dramatically:
$$\text{Stability}_{narcolepsy} \approx 0.2 \text{ (vs 0.95 in healthy)}$$

**Insomnia** (adenosine dysregulation or clock desynchrony):
$$[Adenosine]_{insomnia} = [Adenosine]_{normal} + \Delta[Adenosine]_{elevated}$$

where elevated adenosine alone is insufficient if circadian alerting is also elevated (wake-maintenance zone). Hyperarousal (elevated cortisol, whole-brain glucose metabolism during sleep) prevents proper sleep initiation despite adequate adenosine.

**Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)** (intermittent hypoxia):
$$P_{arousal} = 1 - e^{-k_{hypoxia} \times AHI \times severity}$$

where AHI is apnea-hypopnea index and severity reflects SpO₂ nadir.

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical Value |
|---|---|---|
| $[\text{Orexin}]_{narcolepsy}$ | Orexin in narcolepsy type 1 | <110 pg/mL CSF (vs >200 normal) |
| $[\text{Adenosine}]_{insomnia}$ | Adenosine in insomnia | ~1.5–3 μM at sleep onset (vs 0.5–1.5 normal); insufficient despite elevation |
| Cortisol (insomnia) | Nighttime cortisol in insomnia | Elevated; suppresses sleep-promoting circuits |
| AHI (OSA) | Apnea-hypopnea index | 5–10 (mild); 15–30 (moderate); >30 (severe) |
| SpO₂ nadir (OSA) | Oxygen saturation during apnea | 60–80% (mild); <60% (severe) |
| Arousal threshold (OSA) | EEG arousal triggered by hypoxia/hypercapnia | 20–30 seconds of apnea → EEG microarousal |

**Explanation**: Different sleep disorders involve disruptions at different levels—narcolepsy involves loss of the stabilizing orexin signal; insomnia involves either insufficient adenosine accumulation or excessive circadian alerting (or hyperarousal); OSA involves intermittent hypoxia-triggered arousals fragmenting sleep. Treatment targets the underlying mechanism (orexin agonists for narcolepsy, CBT-I or sleep apnea treatment for insomnia, CPAP for OSA).

### Case Study 11.4: Memory Consolidation Benefit — Motor Learning with and without Sleep

**Scenario**: A subject learns a finger-tapping sequence (motor task). Performance is tested after learning, after 12 hours of wakefulness, and after 8 hours of sleep.

**Calculations**:

Using Section 7.2 (replay coverage) and Section 8.1–8.2 (synaptic changes):

**Performance metric**: Sequence speed (taps per second) and accuracy (% correct).

1. **Immediately after learning** (Session 1):
   - Speed: 4 taps/s, 85% accuracy
   - Synaptic weight change: +0.05 (learning potentiation)
   - Consolidation index: 0 (no consolidation yet)

2. **After 12 h wakefulness** (Session 2, next day):
   - Speed: 4.1 taps/s, 85% accuracy (minimal change; no consolidation)
   - Explanation: Wakefulness does not consolidate motor memories; without sleep, ripple-based replay doesn't occur
   - Synaptic weight: Still +0.05 (same as learning day)
   - Sleep spindles: Low (no SWS) → no spindle-ripple coupling → poor consolidation

3. **After 8 h sleep** (Session 3, one night later):
   - Speed: **4.8 taps/s**, 92% accuracy (significant improvement)
   - Improvement: (+0.8 taps/s) / 4 taps/s = **20% speed gain**
   - Accuracy gain: 7 percentage points
   - Explanation: Sleep provides optimal conditions for motor consolidation
   - Motor cortex synaptic weight during sleep: Re-potentiates memory traces (not downscaled), while non-memory synapses are weakened
   - Ripple-spindle coupling: ~30% of learning-relevant memories replayed per night
   - SWS delta power: High (rebound from prior wakefulness) → high ripple-spindle coordination

4. **After another 12 h wakefulness** (Session 4):
   - Speed: 4.8 taps/s, 92% accuracy (stable; memory consolidated)
   - The sleep-dependent consolidation is now stable against wakefulness

**Quantitative model** (learning gain with vs without sleep):

$$\text{Performance gain} = \text{baseline improvement} \times (1 + k_{sleep} \times \text{SWS quality})$$

- With SWS: gain = 1 × (1 + 0.5 × 1.0) = 1.5× (50% boost)
- Without SWS (deprivation): gain = 1 × (1 + 0.5 × 0) = 1.0× (no boost)

**Speed improvement**:
- Without sleep: 4 → 4.1 taps/s (2.5% "drift"; no real learning)
- With sleep: 4 → 4.8 taps/s (20% consolidation)
- **Sleep benefit for motor learning**: 8× improvement over wakefulness alone

**Conclusion**: Motor learning (and most declarative learning) absolutely requires sleep for consolidation. A single night of sleep produces learning gains equivalent to days of wakefulness practice. This explains why athletes and students prioritize sleep for performance.

### Case Study 11.5: Synaptic Homeostasis — AMPA Receptor Phosphorylation After Sleep Deprivation and Recovery

**Scenario**: Track AMPA receptor phosphorylation (a marker of synaptic strength) across one full day of normal sleep, one day of sleep deprivation, and recovery sleep.

**Calculations**:

Using Section 8 (synaptic potentiation and downscaling):

| Time | Condition | AMPA phos. | Avg synaptic weight | Delta power | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Morning** (7 AM) | Normal | 30% | 0.50 | High (rebound) | Post-sleep baseline |
| **Afternoon** (3 PM) | Normal learning | 45% | 0.58 | Declining | LTP from experience |
| **Evening** (11 PM) | Before sleep | 50% | 0.62 | Low (fatigue) | Peak synaptic strength after day of learning |
| **Next morning** (7 AM after sleep) | Post-sleep | 35% | 0.51 | High (rebound) | **20% downscaling** during night |

**Sleep-deprivation scenario** (no sleep, same day):

| Time | Condition | AMPA phos. | Avg weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 7 AM | Start | 30% | 0.50 | Baseline |
| 3 PM | After learning | 45% | 0.58 | LTP accumulation |
| 11 PM | 16 h awake | 50% | 0.62 | Peak potentiation |
| **7 AM next day** | **24 h awake (no sleep)** | **55%** | **0.65** | **Saturated; can't learn more!** |
| 3 PM (next day) | 40 h awake | 58% | 0.67 | Attempted learning fails |
| 11 PM (next night) | 48 h awake + sleep | 40% | 0.55 | Rebound downscaling (deeper sleep) |

**Quantitative predictions**:

Phosphorylation during wakefulness (potentiation):
$$P(t) = P_0 + k_p \times t_{wake}$$
where $k_p ≈ 2\%$ per hour.

After 16 h: $P = 30 + 2 \times 16 = 62\%$ (vs observed ~50%).

During sleep (downscaling):
$$P(t) = P_{wake} \times e^{-k_d \times t_{sleep}}$$
where $k_d ≈ 0.04$ per hour.

After 8 h sleep: $P = 50 \times e^{-0.32} = 50 \times 0.73 = 36.5\%$ ✓

**LTP saturation capacity** (max new learning per day):
- Normal: Can potentiate ~10–15% of synapses (reserve for new learning)
- After 1 night sleep deprivation: <5% available (impaired learning next day)
- After 2+ nights: Nearly 0% available (cannot form new memories; **sleep deprivation amnesia**)

**Recovery sleep after deprivation**:
- More intense (deeper SWS, higher delta power)
- Downscaling proportionally deeper: 30–40% reduction (vs normal 20%)
- Typically requires 1.5× normal sleep duration to recover capacity fully

**Conclusion**: AMPA receptor phosphorylation and synaptic strength increase linearly during wakefulness (learning). Without sleep-dependent downscaling, synapses saturate within ~24 h, preventing further learning and causing impaired memory. Sleep restores learning capacity and is non-negotiable for cognitive function.

### Case Study 11.6: Obstructive Sleep Apnea — Hypoxia-Induced Arousals

**Scenario**: A 55-year-old man with moderate OSA (AHI = 20, SpO₂ nadir = 75%) experiences repeated arousal-disrupted sleep cycles. Model SpO₂ changes and arousal threshold during apneas.

**Calculations**:

**Typical OSA cycle** (duration ~30–60 seconds):

- **t = 0**: Arousal resolves, airway opens, respiration normal. SpO₂ = 95%, PCO₂ = 40 mmHg
- **t = 10 s**: Airway collapse begins (loss of muscle tone during sleep)
- **t = 20 s**: Full apnea (no airflow). SpO₂ begins to drop
- **t = 30 s**: SpO₂ = 85%, PCO₂ = 50 mmHg (hypercapnia triggers reflex)
- **t = 40 s**: SpO₂ = 75% (nadir), cortical EEG arouses (brief wake)
- **t = 45 s**: Arousal + muscle tone restoration → airway opens
- **t = 50 s**: Hyperpnea (rapid breathing to restore SpO₂)
- **t = 60 s**: SpO₂ recovers to 95%; airway collapses again

**SpO₂ dynamics during apnea** (from normal to nadir):

$$SpO_2(t) = 95 - k_{desat} \times t$$

where $k_{desat} ≈ 0.4 \%$ per second (rate of desaturation, depends on baseline O₂ reserve):

- t = 20 s: SpO₂ = 95 − 0.4 × 20 = 87%
- t = 30 s: SpO₂ = 95 − 0.4 × 30 = 83%
- t = 40 s: SpO₂ = 95 − 0.4 × 40 = 79% ✓ (close to observed 75%)

**PCO₂ increase** (hypercapnia):

$$PCO_2(t) = 40 + k_{CO_2} \times t$$

where $k_{CO_2} ≈ 0.3$ mmHg per second:

- t = 40 s: PCO₂ = 40 + 0.3 × 40 = 52 mmHg (triggers arousal reflex)

**Arousal threshold** (minimum hypoxia/hypercapnia needed to trigger EEG arousal):

Arousal probability:
$$P_{arousal} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\beta(Severity - \Theta_{arousal})}}$$

where Severity = combined hypoxia + hypercapnia signal (arbitrary units).

- Mild apnea (SpO₂ nadir 90%, PCO₂ = 45): Severity = 0.3 → P(arousal) ~20% (some apneas don't arouse)
- Moderate apnea (SpO₂ 75%, PCO₂ 52): Severity = 0.8 → P(arousal) ~95% (most arouse)
- Severe apnea (SpO₂ 60%, PCO₂ 60): Severity = 1.5 → P(arousal) ~99% (nearly all arouse)

**Per-night summary** (AHI = 20 events/hour → ~40 events over 8-hour sleep):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Number of apneas/hypopneas | 40 |
| Arousals (awakenings >3 s) | 35–40 |
| Total arousal-induced wakefulness | 20–30 min (fragmented sleep) |
| SpO₂ nadir (lowest point) | 75% |
| Time SpO₂ <90% | ~200 min (25% of sleep time) |
| Mean SpO₂ overnight | ~88% (vs normal 96%) |

**Sleep architecture disruption**:
- SWS (stage N3): 5–10% (should be 20%) — **severely reduced**
- Rapid sleep-stage cycling (NREM→arousal→wake→N1/N2; prevents reaching N3)
- REM fragmentation (apneas occur in REM, causing arousals)
- **Result**: Almost no consolidation sleep; memory impairment and daytime somnolence

**Consequences of chronic nightly hypoxia/fragmentation**:
- Oxidative stress (recurrent SpO₂ crashes → ROS burst on reoxygenation)
- Intermittent hypoxia-induced microglia activation (neuroinflammation)
- Impaired glymphatic clearance (fragmented sleep blocks Aβ clearance)
- Hippocampal and PFC gray matter atrophy (from chronic hypoxia)
- Hypertension and cardiovascular stress (sympathetic activation during apneas)

**CPAP treatment effect** (eliminates airway collapse):
- Prevents apneas (AHI → 0–5)
- Maintains SpO₂ >94% throughout night
- Allows full duration SWS and REM
- Within 2–4 weeks: memory improves, daytime alertness returns
- Within months: PFC and hippocampal atrophy partially reverses

**Conclusion**: Moderate OSA causes severe sleep fragmentation, preventing restorative deep sleep and consolidation. Chronic nightly hypoxia + sleep fragmentation drives neurodegeneration and cognitive impairment, explaining why untreated OSA is associated with increased dementia risk. CPAP is highly effective at restoring sleep quality and potentially preventing neurodegenerative sequelae.
