# Chapter 14: Sleep Neuroscience

## 14.1 Sleep Architecture

Humans spend approximately one-third of their lives asleep. Sleep is organized into recurring cycles (~90 minutes each; 4–6 cycles per night) comprising two fundamentally distinct states:

### NREM (Non-Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep

**Stage N1** (light sleep, ~5% of total sleep):
- Transition from wakefulness; easily aroused
- EEG: Theta waves (4–8 Hz), vertex sharp waves
- Slow rolling eye movements; decreased muscle tone
- Hypnagogic hallucinations, hypnic jerks (myoclonic twitches)

**Stage N2** (intermediate sleep, ~50% of total sleep):
- **Sleep spindles** (12–16 Hz, 0.5–2 s bursts): Generated by thalamic reticular nucleus → thalamocortical relay neurons → cortex. Functions: protect sleep from external disturbances (sensory gating), promote synaptic plasticity and memory consolidation (Ca²⁺ influx during spindle oscillations → CaMKII activation → LTP). Spindle density correlates with IQ measures and learning ability.
- **K-complexes** (large biphasic waveforms): Evoked by external stimuli or generated spontaneously. Reflect cortical down-states; may serve a sleep-protective function (suppressing arousal) and contribute to slow-wave activity buildup.

**Stage N3** (deep/slow-wave sleep, ~20–25% of total sleep):
- EEG: High-amplitude delta waves (0.5–4 Hz) and slow oscillations (<1 Hz)
- **Slow oscillations** (~0.75 Hz): Consist of alternating UP states (neuronal depolarization and firing) and DOWN states (hyperpolarization and silence). Generated by cortical Layer V pyramidal neurons; propagate as traveling waves across the cortex. UP states provide temporal windows for hippocampal sharp-wave ripples and thalamocortical spindles — the three-way coordination critical for memory consolidation (see Chapter 7).
- Highest arousal threshold; growth hormone release peak
- Predominates in the first half of the night; decreases with age

### REM (Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep (~20–25% of total sleep)

- EEG: Desynchronized, low-amplitude, mixed-frequency (similar to wakefulness); prominent **theta rhythm** in hippocampus; **PGO waves** (pontine-geniculate-occipital — phasic bursts)
- **Rapid eye movements**: Conjugate saccade-like movements, generated by pontine burst neurons
- **Muscle atonia**: Active inhibition of spinal motor neurons via glycinergic/GABAergic circuits originating in the sublaterodorsal nucleus (SLD/SubC) → ventromedial medulla → spinal motor neurons. Loss of REM atonia → REM sleep behavior disorder (acting out dreams — a strong prodromal marker for α-synucleinopathies: Parkinson's, Lewy body dementia)
- **Dreaming**: Most vivid and narrative-rich dreams occur during REM
- **Autonomic instability**: Variable heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing (irregular)
- **Sexual arousal**: Penile erections (males), clitoral engorgement (females) — independent of dream content
- Predominates in the second half of the night

## 14.2 Sleep-Wake Regulation: The Two-Process Model

Sleep timing is governed by two independent processes (Borbély, 1982):

### Process S (Homeostatic Sleep Pressure)

Sleep pressure builds exponentially during wakefulness and dissipates exponentially during sleep (primarily during NREM slow-wave sleep).

**Molecular basis**: **Adenosine** accumulation in the basal forebrain and cortex during wakefulness. Adenosine is a byproduct of ATP metabolism; its concentration increases with sustained neural activity. Adenosine acts on:
- **A1 receptors** (inhibitory): Suppresses wake-promoting neurons in basal forebrain and cortex → promotes sleepiness
- **A2A receptors** (excitatory): Activates sleep-promoting neurons in the ventrolateral preoptic area (VLPO) and nucleus accumbens

**Caffeine** blocks A1 and A2A receptors → counteracts sleep pressure (but does not reduce the actual homeostatic need for sleep — sleep debt accumulates).

Other homeostatic factors: Prostaglandin D₂, cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α — link between infection/inflammation and sleepiness), GABA accumulation, and astrocytic adenosine release.

### Process C (Circadian Alerting Signal)

The SCN generates a circadian rhythm of alerting that opposes sleep pressure during the day. This signal strengthens throughout the day, peaking in the evening ("wake maintenance zone" — the hardest time to fall asleep, ~2–3 hours before habitual bedtime, despite high sleep pressure). The circadian alerting signal drops sharply at night, coinciding with melatonin onset and the opening of the "sleep gate."

The two processes interact: sleep occurs when Process S exceeds Process C threshold; waking occurs when Process S has dissipated sufficiently. Jet lag and shift work create misalignment between S and C.

## 14.3 Neural Circuits of Sleep and Wakefulness

### Wake-Promoting Systems

(See Chapter 8, Section 8.5 for arousal systems table.)

Key wake-promoting nuclei: Locus coeruleus (NE), raphe nuclei (5-HT), tuberomammillary nucleus (histamine), basal forebrain (ACh), lateral hypothalamus (orexin/hypocretin), ventral periaqueductal gray (DA), pedunculopontine/laterodorsal tegmental nuclei (ACh — also REM-on).

### Sleep-Promoting Systems

**Ventrolateral preoptic area (VLPO)**: The primary sleep-promoting nucleus. Contains GABAergic and galaninergic neurons that inhibit all major wake-promoting nuclei during sleep. VLPO neurons are active during NREM sleep; damage → severe insomnia (as in fatal familial insomnia, though this disease primarily involves thalamic degeneration).

**Median preoptic nucleus (MnPO)**: Also contains sleep-active GABAergic neurons; may respond to homeostatic sleep pressure and initiate sleep onset.

**Parafacial zone (PZ)**: GABAergic neurons in the medullary brainstem; promote NREM slow-wave sleep by inhibiting the parabrachial wake-promoting nucleus.

### The Flip-Flop Switch (Saper, 2001)

VLPO and wake-promoting nuclei **mutually inhibit** each other, creating a bistable switch with two stable states (awake or asleep) and rapid transitions between them. **Orexin/hypocretin neurons** stabilize the switch in the wake position by exciting wake-promoting nuclei. Loss of orexin → unstable switch → inappropriate state transitions → **narcolepsy type 1** (excessive daytime sleepiness + cataplexy — sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by emotions).

### REM Sleep Circuitry

**REM-on neurons**: Sublaterodorsal nucleus (SLD/SubC; glutamatergic) — generates REM sleep. Projects to:
- Ventromedial medulla → spinal motor neuron inhibition → atonia
- Basal forebrain → cortical activation
- PPT/LDT → cholinergic activation of cortex

**REM-off neurons**: Ventrolateral periaqueductal gray (vlPAG) and lateral pontine tegmentum (LPT) — GABAergic; inhibit SLD during wakefulness and NREM.

**REM flip-flop**: Mutual inhibition between REM-on (SLD) and REM-off (vlPAG/LPT) neurons creates a second bistable switch within sleep, controlling NREM↔REM transitions. Orexin stabilizes this switch in the REM-off position (explaining why narcolepsy includes frequent inappropriate REM intrusions).

## 14.4 The Glymphatic System

Discovered by Nedergaard and colleagues (2012), the glymphatic system is a brain-wide waste clearance pathway that is most active during sleep.

### Mechanism

1. **CSF enters brain**: Arterial pulsations drive CSF from the subarachnoid space along perivascular (Virchow-Robin) spaces surrounding penetrating arterioles
2. **CSF enters interstitial space**: Passes through **aquaporin-4 (AQP4) water channels** on astrocyte endfeet lining the perivascular space
3. **Convective flow**: CSF mixes with interstitial fluid (ISF), flushing solutes through the brain parenchyma
4. **Waste collection**: ISF and solutes collect along perivenous channels and drain into cervical lymphatic vessels and meningeal lymphatic vessels (rediscovered in 2015 — overturned the dogma that the brain lacked lymphatics)

### Sleep Enhancement

Glymphatic clearance is **~60% more efficient during sleep** compared to wakefulness (and during anesthesia). The mechanism: during sleep, **interstitial space volume increases ~60%** (neurons shrink) due to decreased noradrenergic tone from the locus coeruleus. This expansion reduces resistance to convective flow.

### Clinical Significance

- **Amyloid-β clearance**: Aβ (the amyloid precursor protein fragment that accumulates in Alzheimer's disease) is cleared via the glymphatic system. Sleep deprivation → impaired Aβ clearance → increased Aβ accumulation. This may explain the epidemiological link between chronic sleep disruption and increased Alzheimer's risk.
- **Tau clearance**: Tau protein spread may also be facilitated by disrupted glymphatic flow
- **Traumatic brain injury**: TBI impairs glymphatic function → reduced waste clearance → may contribute to post-traumatic neurodegeneration (CTE)
- **Aging**: Glymphatic function declines with age (AQP4 depolarization from endfeet, arterial stiffening → reduced pulsatile drive) — potentially contributing to age-related protein aggregation disorders

## 14.5 Functions of Sleep

### Memory Consolidation
(See Chapter 7, Section 7.4 for detailed mechanisms.)
NREM slow-wave sleep: Hippocampal-to-cortical transfer of declarative memories via coordinated slow oscillations, spindles, and sharp-wave ripples.
REM sleep: Procedural memory consolidation, emotional memory processing, creative insight.

### Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis (SHY) — Tononi & Cirelli

**Core idea**: Wakefulness is associated with net synaptic potentiation (learning strengthens synapses throughout the day). This increases energy demands, space requirements, and signal-to-noise ratio. NREM slow-wave sleep provides **global synaptic downscaling** — a proportional reduction in synaptic strength that renews learning capacity, reduces energy costs, and improves signal-to-noise.

**Evidence**: Synaptic markers (AMPA receptor phosphorylation, spine size, slope of cortical evoked potentials) increase with wakefulness and decrease with sleep. Sleep deprivation saturates LTP capacity; recovery sleep restores it.

**Refinement**: Not all synapses are downscaled — some are selectively strengthened during sleep (the ones tagged during waking experience for consolidation). The SHY may apply primarily to weak, non-tagged synapses while the active systems consolidation model applies to tagged memory traces.

### Immune Function
Sleep enhances immune function:
- NREM sleep promotes pro-inflammatory cytokine production (IL-1β, TNF-α) and T-cell redistribution to lymph nodes
- Sleep deprivation impairs antibody response to vaccination, reduces natural killer cell activity, and increases susceptibility to infection (sleeping <6 hours → 4.2× increased risk of catching a cold)
- Bidirectional: Infection → increased sleep (via cytokine-mediated activation of sleep circuits)

### Emotional Regulation
REM sleep recalibrates emotional reactivity:
- Overnight REM sleep reduces amygdala response to previously encountered emotional stimuli
- The neurochemical milieu of REM (absent norepinephrine from LC; high acetylcholine) may allow emotional memories to be reprocessed without the arousal signature — "sleeping off" emotional distress
- Sleep deprivation → ~60% increase in amygdala reactivity to negative stimuli, with reduced amygdala-mPFC functional connectivity (impaired top-down regulation)

### Brain Development
Neonates sleep 16–18 hours/day; ~50% REM (vs. ~20% in adults). The high proportion of sleep (especially REM) in early life likely supports:
- Synaptic formation and refinement
- Neural circuit maturation
- Activity-dependent development of sensory systems

## 14.6 Sleep Disorders

### Insomnia
Most prevalent sleep disorder (~10% chronic). Characterized by difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep despite adequate opportunity. Neurobiological model: Hyperarousal — elevated cortisol, increased whole-brain glucose metabolism during NREM sleep (insufficient deactivation of wake circuits), smaller VLPO cell populations. Treatment: Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I, first-line), pharmacotherapy (GABA-A modulators, orexin receptor antagonists, melatonin receptor agonists).

### Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)
Repetitive upper airway collapse during sleep → intermittent hypoxia + sleep fragmentation. Prevalence: ~10–30% of adults. Consequences: excessive daytime sleepiness, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, cognitive impairment (particularly executive function and memory — hippocampal and PFC gray matter reduction), and increased risk of neurodegenerative disease. Treatment: Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP).

### Narcolepsy
**Type 1**: Loss of orexin/hypocretin neurons (autoimmune destruction; strong HLA-DQB1*06:02 association) → excessive daytime sleepiness, cataplexy, sleep paralysis, hypnagogic hallucinations, fragmented nighttime sleep. CSF orexin-A levels <110 pg/mL (vs. >200 pg/mL normal) is diagnostic.
**Type 2**: Similar symptoms without cataplexy; orexin levels usually normal; mechanism unclear.

### REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD)
Loss of normal REM atonia → patients physically act out dreams (often violent or action-filled). >80% of idiopathic RBD patients eventually develop an α-synucleinopathy (Parkinson's disease, Lewy body dementia, or multiple system atrophy) — RBD is the strongest known prodromal marker, often preceding motor symptoms by 10–15 years. Mechanism: Neurodegeneration of brainstem REM atonia circuits (SLD → ventromedial medulla pathway).
