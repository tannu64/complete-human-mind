# Chapter 8: Consciousness and Awareness

## 8.1 Defining Consciousness

Consciousness remains one of the most challenging problems in neuroscience — the "hard problem" (Chalmers, 1995): why and how do physical brain processes give rise to subjective experience (qualia)?

Operational definitions distinguish:

- **Wakefulness (arousal/vigilance)**: The level of consciousness; determined by brainstem ascending reticular activating system (ARAS) and its projections. Can be present without awareness (e.g., vegetative state).
- **Awareness (content of consciousness)**: What one is conscious of — perceptions, thoughts, feelings. Requires intact thalamocortical circuits.
- **Self-awareness (metacognition)**: Awareness of one's own mental states. Associated with medial PFC, anterior insula, and precuneus/PCC.

### States of Consciousness

| State | Arousal | Awareness | EEG Pattern | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alert wakefulness | High | High | Low-amplitude, desynchronized (beta/gamma) | Full conscious experience |
| Relaxed wakefulness | Moderate | Moderate | Alpha (8–13 Hz, posterior) | Eyes closed, resting |
| NREM N1 | Decreasing | Fragmentary | Theta, vertex sharp waves | Hypnagogic imagery, easily aroused |
| NREM N2 | Low | Absent/minimal | Sleep spindles, K-complexes | Most of sleep time (~50%) |
| NREM N3 (slow-wave) | Low | Absent | High-amplitude delta (0.5–4 Hz) | Deepest sleep; hardest to arouse |
| REM sleep | Moderate (internally) | Present (dreaming) | Desynchronized (similar to waking), theta, PGO waves | Muscle atonia, vivid dreams |
| Coma | Absent | Absent | Diffuse slowing or suppression | No sleep-wake cycles |
| Vegetative state (UWS) | Present (eyes open) | Absent | Slow, disorganized | Sleep-wake cycles present; no purposeful behavior |
| Minimally conscious state | Present | Fluctuating/partial | Variable | Intermittent purposeful behavior, visual pursuit |
| Locked-in syndrome | Present | Present | Normal | Complete paralysis except eye movements; fully aware |

## 8.2 Neural Correlates of Consciousness (NCC)

The NCC are defined as the minimal neuronal mechanisms jointly sufficient for any one specific conscious experience (Koch et al., 2016).

### Thalamocortical System

The thalamus and cortex form a recurrent loop that appears essential for conscious experience:

- **Thalamocortical neurons** (relay cells) in specific thalamic nuclei project to layer IV of cortex; cortical layer VI projects back to thalamus; higher-order thalamic nuclei (pulvinar, mediodorsal) relay corticocortical information
- **Thalamic reticular nucleus** (TRN): Shell of GABAergic neurons surrounding the thalamus; provides inhibitory gating — selectively allows or blocks thalamocortical information flow based on attentional demands
- **Intralaminar nuclei**: Diffuse projections to cortex; damage causes severe impairment of consciousness

Evidence for thalamocortical importance:
- Bilateral thalamic lesions (especially intralaminar nuclei) → coma or vegetative state
- General anesthetics at consciousness-abolishing concentrations disrupt thalamocortical connectivity (measured by transcranial magnetic stimulation combined with EEG, TMS-EEG) — the cortex responds but the response doesn't propagate, suggesting loss of effective cortical integration
- Zolpidem (a GABA-A agonist) paradoxically restores consciousness in some vegetative state patients, possibly by normalizing pathological thalamic inhibition

### Posterior Cortical Hot Zone

Koch and colleagues (2016) proposed that the NCC resides primarily in a posterior cortical "hot zone" spanning temporal, parietal, and occipital cortices — the "content-specific NCC." Evidence:
- Direct cortical stimulation of posterior cortex evokes specific conscious experiences (visual, auditory, somatosensory, memory)
- Frontal lobe lesions do not abolish consciousness; extensive posterior lesions can
- Within-trial neural signatures of consciousness (e.g., ignition of late posterior activity ~200–300 ms post-stimulus) distinguish conscious from unconscious processing

The frontal cortex, while important for reporting, monitoring, and acting on conscious content, may not be part of the NCC per se (though this is debated).

## 8.3 Theories of Consciousness

### Global Workspace Theory (GWT) — Baars (1988); Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) — Dehaene, Changeux

**Core idea**: Consciousness arises when information is broadcast globally across the brain via a "global workspace" — a network of long-range cortical connections (primarily involving prefrontal and parietal cortices). Unconscious processing occurs in parallel, modular processors; when a stimulus wins a competition for access to the workspace, it is "ignited" and broadcast widely, becoming conscious.

**Neural signature**: The P3b ERP component (~300–500 ms post-stimulus), accompanied by a "late ignition" of sustained activity in prefrontal and parietal cortex, increased long-range synchrony (especially in beta/gamma band), and non-linear amplification of neural signals (all-or-none ignition, not graded).

**Key predictions**:
- Unconscious processing is feedforward and local; consciousness requires recurrent, global processing
- There is a discrete threshold (ignition) between unconscious and conscious processing
- PFC is essential for consciousness (controversial; see NCC section)

### Integrated Information Theory (IIT) — Tononi (2004, updated)

**Core idea**: Consciousness is identical to integrated information (Φ, "phi") — the amount of information generated by a system above and beyond its parts. A system is conscious to the degree that it is both differentiated (many possible states) and integrated (the whole generates more information than the sum of independent parts).

**Key features**:
- Φ is an intrinsic property of any physical system — it doesn't require an observer
- Consciousness is graded (Φ can be large or small) and structured (the quality of experience corresponds to the informational structure)
- The theory predicts that the posterior cortical hot zone (with its dense recurrent connectivity) has higher Φ than the cerebellum (which has a feedforward, modular architecture despite having more neurons)
- IIT is **substrate-independent** in principle but makes specific predictions: a standard digital computer, despite being able to simulate any function, has low Φ because it operates through sequential logic gates — it would not be conscious regardless of its functional complexity (a controversial prediction)

**Empirical test**: The Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) — derived from TMS-EEG — measures the complexity of the cortical response to a perturbation. PCI reliably discriminates conscious from unconscious states (>0.31 = conscious) in patients with disorders of consciousness, under anesthesia, and during sleep. PCI is correlated with (though not identical to) Φ.

### Higher-Order Theories (HOT)

**Core idea**: A mental state becomes conscious when it is the target of a higher-order representation — a "thought about a thought." First-order sensory representations are unconscious; consciousness requires prefrontal/anterior cortical areas to represent that one is in a particular sensory state.

- **Neural basis**: Lateral PFC, especially dlPFC and anterior PFC, for higher-order monitoring
- **Evidence**: Prefrontal lesions can impair metacognition (confidence judgments) while leaving first-order discrimination intact
- **Criticism**: Extensive frontal damage in humans (lobotomy, bilateral lesions) does not reliably abolish consciousness, contradicting strong HOT predictions

### Recurrent Processing Theory — Lamme (2006)

**Core idea**: Consciousness requires recurrent (feedback) processing between cortical areas. Feedforward processing (from V1 → higher areas) is unconscious; when recurrent loops form (higher → lower cortical areas), the content enters awareness. Does not require frontal involvement.

### Predictive Processing / Active Inference

**Core idea** (Friston, Clark, Hohwy): The brain is fundamentally a prediction machine — it continuously generates top-down predictions about sensory input and updates them via prediction errors (bottom-up signals). Consciousness may arise from the brain's generative model of itself and the world. Perceptual awareness corresponds to the brain's "best guess" about the causes of sensory signals. This framework unifies perception, action, attention, and consciousness under a single principle (free energy minimization / active inference).

## 8.4 Attention

Attention is the selective allocation of processing resources. It is closely related to but dissociable from consciousness (one can be conscious of unattended stimuli and can attend to stimuli without conscious awareness).

### Types of Attention

**Selective (focused) attention**: Filtering relevant from irrelevant information.
- **Biased competition model** (Desimone & Duncan, 1995): Multiple stimuli compete for neural representation; attention biases the competition in favor of behaviorally relevant stimuli via top-down signals from PFC/parietal cortex to sensory cortex (enhancing gain of attended stimulus, suppressing unattended).

**Sustained attention (vigilance)**: Maintaining focus over prolonged periods. Depends on right PFC, right parietal cortex, and noradrenergic system (LC tonic mode). Declines over time (vigilance decrement), accelerated by monotony and sleep deprivation.

**Divided attention**: Simultaneous processing of multiple tasks. Limited by central bottleneck (likely response selection stage involving lateral PFC). Dual-task interference is greater when tasks share processing resources (e.g., two visual tasks interfere more than a visual + auditory task).

### Attention Networks (Posner)

1. **Alerting network**: Maintaining tonic arousal. Locus coeruleus → norepinephrine → right frontal/parietal cortex. Cue: warning signal before target.
2. **Orienting network**: Selecting sensory information. Superior parietal lobule (disengagement), TPJ (reorienting), frontal eye fields (voluntary shift), superior colliculus (overt shift). Cholinergic modulation (basal forebrain).
3. **Executive attention network**: Resolving conflict, error detection. Anterior cingulate cortex, lateral PFC. Dopaminergic modulation.

### Inattentional Blindness and Change Blindness

**Inattentional blindness**: Failure to notice a fully visible but unexpected stimulus when attention is engaged elsewhere (Simons & Chabris, 1999 — "invisible gorilla"). Demonstrates that attention is necessary (though possibly not sufficient) for conscious perception of unexpected stimuli.

**Change blindness**: Failure to detect large changes in a visual scene when the change coincides with a brief disruption (saccade, blink, blank screen). Demonstrates that we do not maintain a detailed conscious representation of the entire visual scene — conscious perception is sparse, constructed "on demand."

## 8.5 Arousal Systems

Consciousness requires a baseline level of arousal maintained by the **ascending reticular activating system (ARAS)** — a collection of brainstem, hypothalamic, and basal forebrain nuclei with diffuse ascending projections:

| System | Source | Transmitter | Wake-Promoting Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Locus coeruleus | Dorsal pons | Norepinephrine | Arousal, attention (silent in REM) |
| Raphe nuclei | Brainstem midline | Serotonin | Tonic arousal (silent in REM) |
| Tuberomammillary nucleus | Posterior hypothalamus | Histamine | Wakefulness (silent in sleep) |
| Pedunculopontine/laterodorsal tegmental | Pons/midbrain | Acetylcholine | Cortical activation, REM sleep |
| Basal forebrain | Substantia innominata, nucleus basalis | Acetylcholine | Cortical activation, attention |
| Ventral periaqueductal gray | Midbrain | Dopamine | Wakefulness |
| Lateral hypothalamus | Hypothalamus | Orexin/hypocretin | Stabilizes wakefulness; loss → narcolepsy |

These systems interact in a **flip-flop switch** model (Saper, 2001): Wake-promoting nuclei mutually inhibit sleep-promoting neurons (VLPO — GABAergic/galaninergic in anterior hypothalamus) and vice versa. Orexin stabilizes the switch in the "wake" position, preventing unwanted transitions. Loss of orexin destabilizes the switch → narcolepsy with cataplexy (sudden sleep/atonia transitions).

## 8.6 General Anesthesia

General anesthesia provides a powerful experimental tool for studying consciousness by reversibly abolishing it.

**Molecular targets**:
- **GABAergic agents** (propofol, barbiturates, volatile agents — sevoflurane, isoflurane): Enhance GABA-A receptor inhibition → suppress cortical excitability
- **NMDA antagonists** (ketamine, nitrous oxide, xenon): Block excitatory glutamatergic transmission
- **α2-adrenergic agonists** (dexmedetomidine): Activate inhibitory receptors in LC → mimics natural NREM sleep pathway

**Neural mechanisms of anesthetic unconsciousness** (converging across different drug types):
1. **Disruption of thalamocortical integration**: Cortex can still respond to stimuli, but the response fails to propagate (reduced effective connectivity measured by TMS-EEG → low PCI)
2. **Disruption of cortical feedback**: Loss of recurrent top-down processing (especially in frontal → posterior direction, measured by directed connectivity analyses)
3. **Hypersynchrony**: Paradoxically, anesthesia can increase synchrony within local cortical areas while abolishing long-range integration — the "fragmentation" of cortical processing
4. **Disruption of subcortical arousal**: Anesthetic effects on brainstem and hypothalamic arousal nuclei contribute, especially for dexmedetomidine

## 8.7 Dreaming

Dreams are conscious experiences occurring primarily (but not exclusively) during REM sleep.

**Neurobiological basis**:
- REM sleep activation: Cholinergic pontine nuclei (PPT/LDT) activate cortex; aminergic systems (LC, raphe, TMN) are silent → explains dream characteristics: vivid imagery, emotional content, poor metacognition, acceptance of bizarre content (reduced prefrontal function + serotonergic silence)
- **PGO (pontine-geniculate-occipital) waves**: Phasic bursts originating in pons → LGN → visual cortex; may generate internal visual imagery during dreams
- Active regions during REM: Amygdala, hippocampus, visual association cortex, medial PFC, anterior cingulate
- Deactivated regions: Dorsolateral PFC (executive control), posterior parietal cortex (directed attention)

**NREM dreaming**: Occurs, but typically less vivid, less bizarre, more thought-like. "Hot zone" posterior cortical activity during NREM correlates with dream experience even in the absence of REM physiology.

**Functions of dreaming** (debated):
- **Threat simulation theory** (Revonsuo): Dreams simulate threatening scenarios to rehearse threat detection/avoidance
- **Memory consolidation**: Dreams reflect hippocampal replay and memory reprocessing
- **Emotional regulation**: REM dreaming processes emotional memories (Walker & van der Helm — reduced amygdala reactivity to emotional stimuli after REM sleep)
- **Overfitted brain hypothesis** (Hoel, 2021): Dreams inject noise into brain networks to prevent overfitting to daily experiences

## 8.8 Free Will and Volition

The neuroscience of volition intersects consciousness research:

**Bereitschaftspotential (readiness potential)**: A slow negative cortical potential beginning ~1–2 seconds before voluntary movement onset, originating in SMA and pre-SMA. Libet's experiment (1983) showed the readiness potential begins ~550 ms before the reported conscious intention to act (~200 ms before movement) — suggesting unconscious neural processes initiate actions before conscious "will."

**Modern reinterpretation**: The readiness potential may reflect stochastic fluctuations in neural activity that cross a threshold, rather than a specific "decision to act." Consciousness may play a role in vetoing (inhibiting) rather than initiating actions. Schurger et al. (2012) modeled the readiness potential as a threshold-crossing process with stochastic neural accumulation, challenging the interpretation that decisions are predetermined.

**Frontal lobe and volition**: Patients with medial frontal lesions (SMA, pre-SMA, ACC) show:
- **Akinetic mutism**: Awake but lack drive to speak or move (extreme abulia)
- **Alien hand syndrome** (medial frontal or callosal): Involuntary purposeful actions by one hand
- **Utilization behavior** (orbitofrontal): Compulsive interaction with nearby objects

These cases dissociate awareness, intention, and action — suggesting that "will" is a multi-component process distributed across frontal networks, not a single central executive.
