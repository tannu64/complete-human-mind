# Chapter 10: Language and Cognition

## 10.1 The Neurobiology of Language

Language is the most uniquely human cognitive faculty, supported by a left-lateralized (in ~95% of right-handers and ~70% of left-handers) network of cortical and subcortical structures.

### Classical Model (Wernicke-Geschwind)

The classical model identified two primary language areas connected by a fiber bundle:

- **Broca's area (BA 44/45, left inferior frontal gyrus)**: Speech production. Damage → **Broca's aphasia**: Non-fluent, effortful speech with preserved comprehension; telegraphic output lacking function words and grammatical morphology ("agrammatism"); patient aware of deficit.
- **Wernicke's area (BA 22, left posterior superior temporal gyrus)**: Language comprehension. Damage → **Wernicke's aphasia**: Fluent but empty speech with neologisms, paraphasias, and impaired comprehension; patient typically unaware of deficit.
- **Arcuate fasciculus**: White matter tract connecting Broca's and Wernicke's areas. Damage → **Conduction aphasia**: Fluent speech and preserved comprehension but impaired repetition; frequent phonemic paraphasias.

### Modern Dual-Stream Model (Hickok & Poeppel, 2007)

The classical model is now recognized as oversimplified. Modern neuroimaging and lesion studies support a dual-stream architecture:

**Ventral stream ("what" pathway) — comprehension**:
- Bilateral superior/middle temporal gyri → anterior temporal lobe (left-dominant for speech)
- Maps sound to meaning (spectrotemporal analysis → phonological → lexical → semantic processing)
- Key areas: Superior temporal sulcus (STS, phonological processing), middle temporal gyrus (lexical-semantic), anterior temporal lobe (combinatorial semantics — combining word meanings into sentence meaning)
- Damage: Word deafness, semantic paraphasia, semantic dementia

**Dorsal stream ("how" pathway) — production and sensorimotor integration**:
- Left posterior superior temporal gyrus/Sylvian parietal-temporal area (Spt) → posterior inferior frontal gyrus (Broca's)
- Maps sound representations to articulatory motor programs (auditory-motor integration)
- Via the arcuate fasciculus and superior longitudinal fasciculus
- Critical for: Speech repetition, phonological working memory, speech monitoring, learning new words
- Damage: Conduction aphasia, apraxia of speech

### Language Beyond Broca and Wernicke

**Supplementary motor area (SMA) and pre-SMA**: Speech initiation; damage → transcortical motor aphasia (non-fluent but preserved repetition; mutism in acute phase).

**Basal ganglia**: Procedural aspects of language — sequencing, rule application, grammatical processing (particularly the caudate nucleus). Lesions → agrammatic production, slow speech.

**Cerebellum**: Timing of speech production, verbal working memory (right cerebellar hemisphere — contralateral to left cortical language areas via cerebro-cerebellar circuits).

**Right hemisphere**: Prosody (emotional tone of speech — right inferior frontal gyrus for production, right temporal cortex for comprehension), discourse-level coherence, pragmatics, humor, metaphor, sarcasm.

**Thalamus**: Left pulvinar and VL/VA nuclei — lesions cause thalamic aphasia (fluctuating alertness, word-finding difficulty, semantic paraphasias).

## 10.2 Reading and Writing

### Neural Basis of Reading

Reading is a culturally invented skill (~5,000 years old) that repurposes evolutionary older visual and language circuits:

**Visual Word Form Area (VWFA)**: Left mid-fusiform gyrus (BA 37) — specializes during literacy acquisition for rapid, automatic recognition of letter strings. Part of the ventral visual stream; positioned between face-selective and object-selective regions. Damage → **pure alexia** (letter-by-letter reading, intact writing).

**Dual-route model**:
- **Lexical route** (ventral): VWFA → anterior/middle temporal cortex → meaning. Direct access to stored word representations; handles familiar words, irregular words (e.g., "yacht")
- **Sublexical/phonological route** (dorsal): VWFA → posterior temporal → inferior parietal lobule (grapheme-to-phoneme conversion) → IFG → pronunciation. Handles novel words, pseudowords (e.g., "blent")

**Dyslexia**: Characterized by difficulty in phonological processing despite adequate intelligence and instruction. Neural basis: Reduced activation and connectivity in left temporoparietal cortex (Spt — grapheme-phoneme conversion region); reduced white matter integrity in left arcuate fasciculus. Genetic basis: Polygenic; associated variants in DCDC2, KIAA0319, DYX1C1 — genes involved in neuronal migration during cortical development.

## 10.3 Executive Function

Executive functions (EFs) are higher-order cognitive processes enabling flexible, goal-directed behavior. Primarily dependent on the **prefrontal cortex** and its connections to basal ganglia, thalamus, and posterior cortical areas.

### Core Executive Functions (Miyake & Friedman, 2012)

**1. Inhibitory control**:
- **Response inhibition**: Suppressing prepotent but inappropriate responses. Neural basis: Right inferior frontal gyrus (IFG) and pre-SMA → subthalamic nucleus (hyperdirect pathway for rapid action cancellation). Measured by stop-signal task, go/no-go task.
- **Interference control**: Resisting distractor interference. Neural basis: dlPFC, ACC (conflict detection → dlPFC implements control).
- **Stroop task**: Naming the ink color of a color word (e.g., "RED" written in blue) — requires inhibiting automatic reading. ACC detects conflict; dlPFC biases processing toward color naming.

**2. Working memory (updating and monitoring)**:
- Actively maintaining and manipulating information. Neural basis: dlPFC (maintenance, manipulation), ventrolateral PFC (selection, comparison), posterior parietal cortex (storage buffer). See Chapter 7 for detailed mechanisms.
- Dopamine modulation: D1 receptor stimulation in dlPFC → sustained firing during delay; inverted-U relationship — too little (fatigue, ADHD) or too much (stress) dopamine impairs working memory.

**3. Cognitive flexibility (set-shifting)**:
- Switching between tasks, rules, or mental sets. Neural basis: dlPFC, ventrolateral PFC, posterior parietal cortex, basal ganglia (striatal update of task rules). Measured by Wisconsin Card Sorting Test, task-switching paradigms.
- Perseveration (inability to shift) is a hallmark of frontal lobe damage.

### Higher-Order Executive Functions

Built from core EFs through combination:
- **Planning**: Decomposing goals into ordered subgoals. Rostral PFC (BA 10, "frontopolar cortex") — manages superordinate goals while subgoals are active; multi-tasking. Tower of London task.
- **Reasoning and problem-solving**: Lateral PFC integrates diverse information; rostral PFC handles abstract, relational reasoning (analogical thinking).
- **Fluid intelligence**: Strongly correlated with working memory capacity and executive function. Neural basis: Lateral PFC + parietal cortex (frontoparietal network); the "multiple-demand system" (Duncan) — a set of regions activated by diverse cognitive demands.

## 10.4 Decision-Making

### Value-Based Decision-Making

**Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)**: Represents the subjective value of different options in a common currency — integrating taste, smell, texture, social context, and expected outcomes into a scalar value signal. OFC neurons encode economic value (demonstrated by single-neuron recordings in monkeys performing choices). Damage: poor real-world decisions despite preserved IQ (Iowa Gambling Task deficit — failure to learn from negative outcomes).

**Ventromedial PFC (vmPFC)**: Overlapping with OFC; encodes expected value, compares options. Works with anterior insula (encodes risk and uncertainty) and ACC (monitors outcomes, detects errors).

**Drift Diffusion Model**: Evidence for each option accumulates over time (noisy integration) until a decision threshold is reached. Speed-accuracy tradeoff is controlled by threshold height. Neural accumulation occurs in lateral intraparietal area (LIP — for perceptual decisions) and ventral striatum/vmPFC (for value-based decisions).

### Dual-Process Theory (Kahneman)

- **System 1** (fast, automatic, intuitive): Amygdala, ventromedial PFC, basal ganglia (habitual responses). Operates via somatic markers, heuristics, pattern matching.
- **System 2** (slow, deliberate, analytical): Dorsolateral PFC, anterior cingulate, posterior parietal cortex. Requires working memory and effortful attention.

Most daily decisions use System 1; System 2 engages when errors are detected (ACC conflict signal) or when the situation demands careful analysis.

### Moral Decision-Making

**Trolley dilemma** studies reveal dissociable neural systems:
- **Personal (emotional) moral dilemmas** (push someone off bridge): Engage vmPFC, amygdala, medial PFC → emotional/intuitive judgment against harmful action
- **Impersonal (cognitive) moral dilemmas** (flip a switch): Engage dlPFC, inferior parietal lobule → utilitarian cost-benefit analysis

Patients with vmPFC lesions show abnormally utilitarian moral judgments — they endorse personally harmful actions more readily, suggesting intact utilitarian reasoning without the normal emotional "brake."

## 10.5 Creativity

Creativity — generating novel, useful ideas — involves dynamic interplay between brain networks:

**Default mode network (DMN)**: Spontaneous idea generation, mind-wandering, associative thinking, mental simulation. Creative people show stronger DMN connectivity.

**Executive control network (ECN/CEN)**: Evaluating, refining, and selecting among generated ideas; maintaining focus on creative goals.

**Salience network**: Toggling between DMN (generation) and ECN (evaluation).

**Key finding**: Highly creative individuals show atypically strong **functional connectivity between DMN and ECN** — networks that are normally anti-correlated in most people. This may enable simultaneous generation and evaluation of novel ideas.

**Divergent thinking** (many possible solutions — "think of all possible uses for a brick") engages bilateral PFC, temporal cortex (remote semantic associations), and ACC. **Convergent thinking** (one correct solution — insight problems) involves right anterior temporal cortex (for sudden remote associations — the "aha!" moment) and ACC (detecting when an insightful solution breaks through).

**Neurotransmitter involvement**: Moderate dopamine levels in PFC promote creative flexibility; too high or too low impairs it (inverted-U). The link between creativity and psychosis-proneness may relate to shared dopaminergic characteristics (reduced latent inhibition — reduced filtering of "irrelevant" information → more raw material for novel associations).

## 10.6 Numerical Cognition

**Intraparietal sulcus (IPS)**: The core neural substrate for numerical magnitude representation ("number sense"). Bilateral IPS activation during numerical comparison, estimation, and mental arithmetic. Contains neurons tuned to specific numerosities (demonstrated in single-neuron recordings in macaques). Damage → **acalculia** (impaired number processing).

**Triple-code model** (Dehaene):
1. **Analog magnitude representation** (bilateral IPS): Approximate, non-verbal sense of quantity; present in infants and many animal species
2. **Visual Arabic number form** (left fusiform, bilateral occipitotemporal): Recognizing written digits
3. **Verbal code** (left perisylvian language areas): Number words, memorized arithmetic facts (multiplication tables — retrieved from left angular gyrus via verbal memory)

**Math anxiety**: Activates pain-related regions (bilateral dorso-posterior insula); reduces working memory resources available for mathematical processing via rumination. Not related to math ability per se but to anticipated distress.

## 10.7 Music and the Brain

Music processing engages distributed bilateral networks:

**Pitch/melody**: Right superior temporal gyrus (right-lateralized for pitch processing), Heschl's gyrus, right anterior temporal lobe
**Rhythm/timing**: Basal ganglia (especially putamen), cerebellum, SMA, premotor cortex
**Harmony**: Inferior frontal gyrus (syntax-like processing of harmonic structure; shared resources with linguistic syntax — Broca's area homolog in right hemisphere)
**Emotional response to music**: Nucleus accumbens (chills/pleasure), amygdala, insula, vmPFC, hypothalamus (autonomic responses)

**Musical training effects**: Enlarged corpus callosum (especially anterior portion), increased gray matter in motor cortex (hand area), auditory cortex, and cerebellum; enhanced auditory processing; stronger phonological awareness (benefiting language skills). Training before age 7 produces particularly robust structural changes (sensitive period for auditory-motor integration).

**Amusia (tone-deafness)**: Congenital (~4% prevalence) or acquired. Involves disrupted connectivity between right auditory cortex and right IFG (via right arcuate fasciculus).
