# Chapter 2: Cellular Neuroscience

## 2.1 The Neuron: Structure and Classification

### Basic Neuronal Architecture

The neuron is the fundamental computational unit of the nervous system. Each neuron consists of:

**Soma (Cell Body)**: Contains the nucleus with DNA, rough endoplasmic reticulum (Nissl bodies — active protein synthesis), Golgi apparatus, mitochondria, and the cytoskeleton. The soma integrates incoming signals and determines whether an action potential is initiated at the **axon hillock** (the trigger zone with the highest density of voltage-gated Na+ channels, and thus the lowest threshold for firing).

**Dendrites**: Highly branched processes that receive synaptic input. Dendritic surfaces are covered with **dendritic spines** — small protrusions (0.5–2 µm) that serve as postsynaptic compartments. A single cortical pyramidal neuron may have 5,000–30,000 spines. Spine morphology (thin, stubby, mushroom-shaped) correlates with synaptic strength and plasticity state. Spines contain their own signaling machinery: AMPA and NMDA receptors, actin cytoskeleton, smooth endoplasmic reticulum (for local Ca²⁺ stores), and polyribosomes for local protein synthesis. Dendritic computation is not merely passive summation — dendrites exhibit active properties including dendritic spikes (Na⁺, Ca²⁺, and NMDA spikes) that significantly amplify and process inputs.

**Axon**: A single elongated process that conducts action potentials from the soma to synaptic terminals. Axons range from <1 mm (local interneurons) to >1 meter (corticospinal motor neurons). The axon begins at the **axon hillock**, proceeds through the **initial segment** (highest density of Nav1.6 channels), and may branch into **collaterals**. The axon terminal (**bouton**) contains synaptic vesicles filled with neurotransmitter, mitochondria for local ATP production, and the molecular machinery for vesicle docking, fusion, and recycling.

**Myelin Sheath**: Formed by **oligodendrocytes** in the CNS (each myelinating segments of up to 40 axons) and **Schwann cells** in the PNS (each myelinating one segment of one axon). Myelin is ~80% lipid, 20% protein (including myelin basic protein and proteolipid protein). It wraps the axon in multiple concentric layers, with gaps called **nodes of Ranvier** (1–2 µm wide, every 0.2–2 mm) where voltage-gated Na⁺ channels cluster at high density (~1,200/µm²). This enables **saltatory conduction** — the action potential jumps between nodes, increasing conduction velocity from ~0.5–2 m/s (unmyelinated) to ~3–120 m/s (myelinated) while reducing metabolic cost.

### Neuronal Classification

**By morphology:**
- **Unipolar**: Single process (rare in vertebrate CNS; common in invertebrates)
- **Bipolar**: Two processes (one axon, one dendrite) — found in retina, olfactory epithelium, vestibular ganglion
- **Pseudo-unipolar**: Single bifurcating process — dorsal root ganglion sensory neurons
- **Multipolar**: Multiple dendrites, one axon — most common in CNS (pyramidal cells, Purkinje cells, motor neurons)

**By function:**
- **Sensory (afferent) neurons**: Transduce environmental stimuli into neural signals
- **Motor (efferent) neurons**: Innervate muscles and glands (alpha motor neurons, autonomic neurons)
- **Interneurons**: Local circuit neurons; ~20–30% of cortical neurons; predominantly GABAergic inhibitory

**By neurotransmitter:**
- **Glutamatergic**: ~80% of cortical neurons (excitatory pyramidal cells)
- **GABAergic**: ~20% of cortical neurons (inhibitory interneurons — basket cells, chandelier cells, Martinotti cells, VIP interneurons, etc.)
- **Cholinergic, dopaminergic, serotonergic, noradrenergic**: Modulatory neurons with cell bodies in specific brainstem/basal forebrain nuclei

**By firing pattern:**
- **Regular spiking**: Most excitatory cortical neurons
- **Fast spiking**: Parvalbumin-positive (PV+) basket cells — fire at >200 Hz
- **Intrinsically bursting**: Layer V pyramidal cells
- **Low-threshold spiking**: Somatostatin-positive (SST+) interneurons

### Special Neuron Types
- **Purkinje cells**: Cerebellar cortex output; massive planar dendritic trees; GABAergic
- **Betz cells**: Largest neurons in the CNS (soma ~100 µm); layer V primary motor cortex; contribute to corticospinal tract
- **Von Economo neurons (spindle neurons)**: Large bipolar neurons in anterior cingulate and insular cortex; associated with social cognition; found only in great apes, elephants, whales, and humans
- **Mirror neurons**: Originally described in macaque premotor cortex (F5); fire both during action execution and observation of similar actions; debated functional role in humans
- **Place cells, grid cells, head direction cells**: Specialized hippocampal/entorhinal neurons encoding spatial information (see Chapter 7)

## 2.2 Glial Cells

Glial cells ("glia" = glue) are non-neuronal cells that constitute roughly half the cells in the brain (~85 billion). Far from passive support cells, glia actively regulate neural computation, synaptic transmission, and brain homeostasis.

### Astrocytes

The most abundant glial cell type in the CNS. Human astrocytes are significantly larger and more complex than those of other mammals — a single human protoplasmic astrocyte can contact ~2 million synapses (vs. ~100,000 in rodents).

**Functions:**
- **Metabolic support**: Provide lactate to neurons via the astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle (ANLS). Astrocytes take up glucose from blood, convert it to lactate via glycolysis, export lactate through MCT1/4 transporters, and neurons import it via MCT2 for oxidative metabolism.
- **Neurotransmitter recycling**: Astrocytes rapidly clear glutamate from the synaptic cleft via EAAT1/EAAT2 transporters, convert it to glutamine via glutamine synthetase, and shuttle glutamine back to neurons (glutamate-glutamine cycle). This prevents excitotoxicity.
- **Ion homeostasis**: Buffer extracellular K⁺ concentration through Kir4.1 channels and gap junction-connected astrocytic networks (spatial buffering).
- **Blood-brain barrier maintenance**: Astrocyte endfeet cover ~99% of the cerebral vasculature, inducing and maintaining tight junction proteins in endothelial cells.
- **Tripartite synapse**: Astrocytes form an integral part of synaptic signaling. They respond to neurotransmitters (via metabotropic receptors), exhibit intracellular Ca²⁺ waves, and release **gliotransmitters** (glutamate, D-serine, ATP) that modulate synaptic transmission and plasticity.
- **Synapse formation and pruning**: Secrete thrombospondins (promoting synaptogenesis) and participate in complement-mediated synaptic elimination (C1q/C3 tagging).

### Oligodendrocytes

Myelinating cells of the CNS. Each oligodendrocyte extends multiple processes, each wrapping a different axon segment.

**Functions:**
- Myelin formation and maintenance (saltatory conduction)
- Metabolic support to axons: Supply lactate/pyruvate through myelin via MCT1 transporters
- Regulation of axonal caliber and survival

**Oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs / NG2 glia)**: Constitute 5–8% of all CNS cells in adults. Capable of differentiating into new oligodendrocytes throughout life (adaptive myelination). OPCs also receive direct synaptic input from neurons — the only glial cell type known to do so — though the functional significance remains under investigation.

### Microglia

The resident immune cells of the CNS, derived from yolk sac myeloid progenitors that colonize the brain during embryonic development. They constitute ~5–12% of all brain cells.

**Resting (surveilling) state**: Highly ramified morphology with constantly motile processes that survey the local environment. Processes contact neuronal synapses every few hours, sampling the extracellular milieu.

**Activated state**: In response to injury, infection, or neurodegeneration, microglia retract their processes and adopt an amoeboid morphology. They release pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6), reactive oxygen species, and nitric oxide. Activated microglia phagocytose cellular debris, pathogens, and damaged synapses.

**Functions beyond immunity:**
- **Synaptic pruning** during development: Microglia eliminate excess synapses via complement-mediated phagocytosis (C1q → C3 → CR3 receptor on microglia). Dysregulation of this process is implicated in schizophrenia and autism.
- **Neurogenesis regulation**: Modulate adult hippocampal neurogenesis
- **Experience-dependent plasticity**: Activity-dependent synapse elimination in visual cortex during critical periods
- **Neuroinflammation**: Chronic microglial activation is implicated in Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and ALS (see Chapter 15)

### Ependymal Cells

Line the ventricles and central canal of the spinal cord. Their cilia beat in a coordinated fashion to circulate CSF. Specialized ependymal cells in the **choroid plexus** form the blood-CSF barrier and actively secrete CSF. **Tanycytes**, a specialized ependymal subtype lining the third ventricle floor, sense CSF composition and regulate hypothalamic neuroendocrine function.

### Schwann Cells (PNS Only)

The PNS equivalent of oligodendrocytes. Two types:
- **Myelinating Schwann cells**: One cell per internode; facilitate saltatory conduction in peripheral nerves
- **Non-myelinating (Remak) Schwann cells**: Ensheath multiple small-diameter unmyelinated axons (C-fibers)

After peripheral nerve injury, Schwann cells dedifferentiate, clear myelin debris (along with macrophages), and form **bands of Büngner** — guidance tubes that promote axon regeneration. This regenerative capacity is largely absent in the CNS, partly because oligodendrocytes and CNS myelin contain inhibitory molecules (Nogo-A, MAG, OMgp).

### Satellite Glial Cells (PNS Only)

Surround neuronal cell bodies in peripheral ganglia (DRG, autonomic ganglia). Regulate the microenvironment of sensory and autonomic neuron somas; implicated in chronic pain signaling.

## 2.3 Neurogenesis

### Embryonic Neurogenesis
During development, neural stem cells in the **ventricular zone** and **subventricular zone** undergo massive proliferation, generating all neurons of the brain. Peak neurogenesis occurs between gestational weeks 5–25. Radial glial cells serve as both neural progenitors and scaffolds guiding migrating neurons to their cortical positions (inside-out laminar development: deeper layers form first, superficial layers later).

### Adult Neurogenesis
Two well-established neurogenic niches persist in the adult mammalian brain:

1. **Subgranular zone (SGZ)** of the hippocampal dentate gyrus: Neural stem cells generate new granule cells that integrate into hippocampal circuits. Estimated production: ~700 new neurons/day in adult humans (though this number declines with age). New neurons exhibit enhanced plasticity during a ~4–6 week maturation window, characterized by lower LTP thresholds and increased excitability. Adult hippocampal neurogenesis is regulated by:
   - **Enhancers**: Exercise, enriched environment, learning, serotonin, BDNF
   - **Suppressors**: Chronic stress (cortisol), aging, inflammation, sleep deprivation

2. **Subventricular zone (SVZ)** of the lateral ventricles: Generates neuroblasts that migrate along the rostral migratory stream to the olfactory bulb, differentiating into interneurons. In humans, SVZ neurogenesis is robust in infancy but diminishes substantially by adulthood; some evidence suggests SVZ-derived neurons may also contribute to striatal interneuron populations.

**Note**: Adult neurogenesis in humans remains an area of active debate. Some studies (Sorrells et al., 2018) reported minimal hippocampal neurogenesis in adults, while others (Moreno-Jiménez et al., 2019; Tobin et al., 2019) demonstrated ongoing neurogenesis with improved methodologies. The consensus as of 2025 supports continued adult hippocampal neurogenesis, though at lower rates than in rodents.

## 2.4 The Neuronal Cytoskeleton

The neuronal cytoskeleton is essential for maintaining cell shape, axonal transport, and synaptic function:

- **Microtubules**: Hollow tubes of α/β-tubulin dimers; serve as tracks for molecular motor proteins. **Kinesin** moves cargo anterogradely (soma → terminal); **dynein** moves cargo retrogradely (terminal → soma). In axons, microtubules have uniform polarity (plus-ends distal); in dendrites, mixed polarity. **Tau protein** stabilizes axonal microtubules; hyperphosphorylated tau forms neurofibrillary tangles in Alzheimer's disease (see Chapter 15).

- **Neurofilaments**: Intermediate filaments (NF-L, NF-M, NF-H) that determine axon caliber, which in turn influences conduction velocity.

- **Actin filaments (F-actin)**: Enriched in dendritic spines, growth cones, and presynaptic terminals. Dynamic actin remodeling underlies spine morphology changes during synaptic plasticity (LTP → spine enlargement; LTD → spine shrinkage).

## 2.5 Axonal Transport

Neurons are extremely polarized cells; some axons extend over a meter from the soma. Material transport along the axon is essential for neuronal survival and function:

| Type | Speed | Direction | Motor | Cargo |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fast anterograde | 200–400 mm/day | Soma → terminal | Kinesin | Synaptic vesicle precursors, mitochondria, membrane proteins |
| Slow anterograde | 0.2–8 mm/day | Soma → terminal | Kinesin (intermittent) | Cytoskeletal proteins, soluble enzymes |
| Fast retrograde | 150–300 mm/day | Terminal → soma | Dynein | Endosomes, signaling endosomes (e.g., NGF-TrkA), autophagosomes |

Disruption of axonal transport is implicated in multiple neurodegenerative diseases: mutations in kinesin (hereditary spastic paraplegia), dynein/dynactin (motor neuron disease), and tau hyperphosphorylation (Alzheimer's) all compromise transport efficiency.
