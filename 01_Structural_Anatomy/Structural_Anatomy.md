# Chapter 1: Structural Anatomy of the Human Brain

## 1.1 Gross Anatomy and Organizational Principles

The human brain is divided into three primary embryological divisions that persist as major structural regions in the adult: the **prosencephalon** (forebrain), **mesencephalon** (midbrain), and **rhombencephalon** (hindbrain). The forebrain further differentiates into the **telencephalon** (cerebral hemispheres) and **diencephalon** (thalamus, hypothalamus, epithalamus, subthalamus). The hindbrain divides into the **metencephalon** (pons and cerebellum) and **myelencephalon** (medulla oblongata).

### The Cerebrum

The cerebrum constitutes approximately 85% of total brain weight and is divided into two hemispheres connected by the **corpus callosum** — the largest white matter commissure, containing approximately 200–300 million axons. The cerebral surface is highly convoluted, with **gyri** (ridges) and **sulci** (grooves) that increase cortical surface area to approximately 2,500 cm². The cortical thickness ranges from 1.5 mm (in primary visual cortex) to 4.5 mm (in primary motor cortex).

### The Cerebellum

The cerebellum ("little brain") sits posterior to the brainstem, containing more neurons than the rest of the brain combined (~69 billion granule cells alone). Despite comprising only ~10% of brain volume, it contains over 50% of all brain neurons. It is organized into three functional divisions:

- **Vestibulocerebellum** (flocculonodular lobe): Balance and eye movements
- **Spinocerebellum** (vermis and intermediate zones): Limb and trunk motor execution
- **Cerebrocerebellum** (lateral hemispheres): Motor planning, cognition, and possibly language

The cerebellar cortex has a stereotyped three-layer architecture: the molecular layer, the Purkinje cell layer (containing the sole output neurons of the cerebellar cortex), and the granule cell layer. Purkinje cells are among the largest neurons in the brain, with elaborate dendritic arbors receiving up to 200,000 synaptic inputs each.

### The Brainstem

The brainstem connects the cerebrum and cerebellum to the spinal cord, housing critical nuclei for cranial nerves (III–XII), autonomic regulation, and consciousness:

- **Midbrain (Mesencephalon)**: Contains the superior and inferior colliculi (visual and auditory reflexes), the substantia nigra (dopamine production, motor control), the ventral tegmental area (VTA; reward signaling), and the periaqueductal gray (pain modulation).
- **Pons**: Relays information between cortex and cerebellum; contains nuclei for cranial nerves V–VIII; houses the locus coeruleus (primary norepinephrine source) and raphe nuclei (serotonin).
- **Medulla Oblongata**: Controls vital autonomic functions — cardiovascular center (heart rate, blood pressure), respiratory center (breathing rhythm), and the area postrema (chemoreceptor trigger zone for vomiting). Contains the pyramidal decussation where ~90% of corticospinal motor fibers cross.

## 1.2 Cerebral Cortex: Lobes and Functional Areas

The cerebral cortex is organized into four main lobes per hemisphere, each with distinct functional specializations:

### Frontal Lobe (Anterior to Central Sulcus)
- **Primary motor cortex (M1, Brodmann area 4)**: Somatotopic motor output (motor homunculus); upper motor neuron cell bodies for voluntary movement
- **Premotor cortex (BA 6)**: Motor planning and preparation; mirror neuron populations
- **Supplementary motor area (SMA)**: Internally generated movement sequences; bimanual coordination
- **Prefrontal cortex (PFC)**: Executive function, working memory, decision-making, social behavior
  - *Dorsolateral PFC (BA 9, 46)*: Working memory, cognitive flexibility
  - *Ventromedial PFC (BA 10, 11, 25)*: Emotional regulation, value-based decision-making
  - *Orbitofrontal cortex (BA 11, 47)*: Reward evaluation, impulse control
- **Broca's area (BA 44, 45)**: Speech production and language processing (typically left hemisphere)
- **Frontal eye fields (BA 8)**: Voluntary saccadic eye movements

### Parietal Lobe (Between Central and Parieto-occipital Sulci)
- **Primary somatosensory cortex (S1, BA 1, 2, 3)**: Somatotopic representation of touch, pressure, temperature, proprioception (sensory homunculus)
- **Superior parietal lobule (BA 5, 7)**: Sensorimotor integration, spatial orientation, reaching
- **Inferior parietal lobule**: Includes the **supramarginal gyrus (BA 40)** — phonological processing, and the **angular gyrus (BA 39)** — semantic processing, arithmetic, reading
- **Posterior parietal cortex**: Spatial attention, visuomotor transformation, the "where/how" pathway

### Temporal Lobe (Below Lateral Sulcus)
- **Primary auditory cortex (A1, BA 41, 42)**: Tonotopic processing of sound frequency
- **Wernicke's area (BA 22, posterior superior temporal gyrus)**: Language comprehension (typically left hemisphere)
- **Inferior temporal cortex (BA 20, 37)**: Object recognition, face processing (fusiform face area — BA 37)
- **Medial temporal lobe**: Houses the hippocampal formation and parahippocampal gyrus — critical for declarative memory formation
- **Superior temporal sulcus**: Social perception, biological motion, audiovisual integration

### Occipital Lobe (Posterior)
- **Primary visual cortex (V1, BA 17)**: Retinotopic processing; orientation, spatial frequency, color columns
- **Secondary visual areas (V2–V5)**: Progressively complex feature extraction
  - *V2*: Border ownership, illusory contours
  - *V3*: Dynamic form processing
  - *V4*: Color constancy, form recognition
  - *V5/MT*: Motion perception
- **Dorsal stream ("where/how")**: V1 → V5 → posterior parietal cortex — spatial location, motion-guided action
- **Ventral stream ("what")**: V1 → V4 → inferior temporal cortex — object identity, face recognition

## 1.3 Subcortical Structures

### Thalamus
The thalamus is the principal relay station of the brain, comprising ~60 nuclei organized into groups. Nearly all sensory information (except olfaction) passes through thalamic nuclei before reaching the cortex:

- **Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)**: Visual relay → V1
- **Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN)**: Auditory relay → A1
- **Ventral posterolateral/posteromedial nuclei (VPL/VPM)**: Somatosensory relay → S1
- **Pulvinar**: Attention and visual salience
- **Mediodorsal nucleus**: Relay to prefrontal cortex — executive function
- **Anterior nuclei**: Part of the Papez circuit — memory and emotion
- **Reticular nucleus**: Inhibitory gating of thalamic relay — attentional filtering

### Hypothalamus
Despite weighing only ~4 grams, the hypothalamus is the master regulator of homeostasis. It controls the endocrine system via the pituitary gland and integrates autonomic, endocrine, and behavioral responses:

- **Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)**: Master circadian clock; receives direct retinal input
- **Paraventricular nucleus (PVN)**: CRH release (stress response); oxytocin and vasopressin synthesis
- **Supraoptic nucleus**: Vasopressin (ADH) and oxytocin production
- **Arcuate nucleus**: Appetite regulation (NPY/AgRP hunger neurons; POMC/CART satiety neurons); GnRH release
- **Ventromedial hypothalamus**: Satiety center; defensive behavior
- **Lateral hypothalamus**: Hunger/feeding drive; contains orexin/hypocretin neurons (wakefulness)
- **Preoptic area**: Thermoregulation; sleep promotion (VLPO); sexual behavior

### Hippocampal Formation
Located within the medial temporal lobe, the hippocampus is a curved, seahorse-shaped structure essential for declarative memory. Its internal circuit follows a trisynaptic pathway:

1. **Entorhinal cortex (EC)** → **Dentate gyrus (DG)** via the perforant path
2. **DG** → **CA3** via mossy fibers
3. **CA3** → **CA1** via Schaffer collaterals (the classic LTP preparation)
4. **CA1** → **Subiculum** → back to EC and to other cortical regions

The hippocampus also contains specialized spatial cells: **place cells** (CA1/CA3), **grid cells** (entorhinal cortex), **head direction cells**, and **border cells** — forming a cognitive map of the environment.

### Amygdala
The amygdala is an almond-shaped nuclear complex with ~13 nuclei, broadly divided into:
- **Basolateral complex (BLA)**: Receives sensory input; assigns emotional valence; critical for fear conditioning
- **Central nucleus (CeA)**: Primary output; projects to hypothalamus, brainstem — drives autonomic and behavioral fear responses
- **Medial nucleus**: Processes pheromonal/olfactory signals; reproductive behavior

### Basal Ganglia
A group of interconnected subcortical nuclei critical for action selection, habit formation, and reward learning:
- **Striatum**: Divided into caudate nucleus and putamen (dorsal striatum — motor/cognitive) and nucleus accumbens (ventral striatum — reward/motivation). Contains ~95% medium spiny neurons (MSNs) expressing either D1 (direct pathway — movement facilitation) or D2 (indirect pathway — movement suppression) dopamine receptors.
- **Globus pallidus**: Internal segment (GPi) — primary basal ganglia output; external segment (GPe) — part of indirect pathway
- **Subthalamic nucleus (STN)**: Excitatory glutamatergic drive on GPi; target for deep brain stimulation in Parkinson's
- **Substantia nigra**: Pars compacta (SNc) — dopaminergic input to striatum; pars reticulata (SNr) — GABAergic output parallel to GPi

The classic rate model describes two pathways: the **direct pathway** (cortex → striatum D1 → GPi → thalamus — disinhibition, promoting movement) and the **indirect pathway** (cortex → striatum D2 → GPe → STN → GPi → thalamus — increased inhibition, suppressing movement). The **hyperdirect pathway** (cortex → STN → GPi) enables rapid action cancellation.

## 1.4 Limbic System

The limbic system is a conceptual grouping of structures involved in emotion, motivation, memory, and olfaction. Key components include:

- **Hippocampus**: Memory formation and spatial navigation
- **Amygdala**: Emotional processing and fear
- **Cingulate cortex**: Anterior cingulate (ACC) — conflict monitoring, pain affect, motivation; posterior cingulate (PCC) — self-referential processing, default mode network hub
- **Fornix**: Major white matter tract from hippocampus to mammillary bodies
- **Mammillary bodies**: Memory relay station (Papez circuit); damage causes anterograde amnesia
- **Septal nuclei**: Reward, pleasure, cholinergic input to hippocampus
- **Insular cortex**: Interoception (internal body state awareness), disgust, empathy, pain

## 1.5 Ventricular System and Meninges

### Ventricles
The brain contains four fluid-filled ventricles producing cerebrospinal fluid (CSF):
- **Lateral ventricles** (paired, one per hemisphere) — largest; C-shaped
- **Third ventricle** — midline, between the thalami
- **Fourth ventricle** — between pons/cerebellum and medulla

CSF is produced primarily by the **choroid plexus** at a rate of ~500 mL/day (total volume ~150 mL, replaced 3–4 times daily). CSF flows from lateral ventricles → interventricular foramina of Monro → third ventricle → cerebral aqueduct of Sylvius → fourth ventricle → subarachnoid space via foramina of Luschka and Magendie → reabsorbed at arachnoid granulations into dural venous sinuses.

CSF functions: buoyancy (reduces effective brain weight from 1,400 g to ~50 g), cushioning, waste removal, nutrient transport, and chemical signaling.

### Meninges
Three protective membranes surround the brain and spinal cord:
1. **Dura mater** (outermost): Tough, fibrous; forms falx cerebri, tentorium cerebelli, dural venous sinuses
2. **Arachnoid mater** (middle): Avascular; connected to pia by trabeculae spanning subarachnoid space
3. **Pia mater** (innermost): Thin, highly vascularized; adheres directly to brain surface contours

## 1.6 Major White Matter Tracts

White matter consists of myelinated axon bundles connecting brain regions. Major tracts include:

### Commissural (Interhemispheric)
- **Corpus callosum**: Largest commissure (~200–300 million fibers); divided into rostrum, genu, body, and splenium
- **Anterior commissure**: Connects temporal lobes and olfactory structures
- **Hippocampal commissure (psalterium)**: Connects hippocampal formations

### Association (Intrahemispheric)
- **Arcuate fasciculus / Superior longitudinal fasciculus**: Connects frontal and temporal language areas (Broca's ↔ Wernicke's)
- **Inferior longitudinal fasciculus**: Connects occipital and temporal lobes — visual object recognition
- **Uncinate fasciculus**: Connects orbitofrontal cortex to anterior temporal lobe — emotional labeling of memories
- **Cingulum**: Runs within cingulate gyrus — connects limbic structures

### Projection (Cortex ↔ Subcortical)
- **Internal capsule**: Major highway; anterior limb (frontopontine fibers), genu (corticobulbar), posterior limb (corticospinal tract, thalamocortical radiations)
- **Corona radiata**: Fan-shaped projection fibers spreading from internal capsule to cortex
- **Optic radiations**: LGN to primary visual cortex
- **Corticospinal tract**: Primary motor cortex → spinal cord motor neurons (pyramidal tract)

## 1.7 Cytoarchitectonic Organization

The cerebral cortex is organized into six histological layers (neocortex), numbered from the surface inward:

| Layer | Name | Primary Cell Types | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Molecular | Few neurons; apical dendrites, horizontal axons | Dendritic integration zone |
| II | External granular | Small pyramidal and stellate cells | Corticocortical input |
| III | External pyramidal | Medium pyramidal cells | Corticocortical output (association & commissural) |
| IV | Internal granular | Stellate (granule) cells | Thalamocortical input (thick in sensory areas) |
| V | Internal pyramidal | Large pyramidal cells (including Betz cells in M1) | Subcortical output (corticospinal, corticostriatal) |
| VI | Multiform/Polymorphic | Diverse neuron types | Corticothalamic feedback |

Brodmann's classification (1909) identified 52 distinct cytoarchitectonic areas based on variations in this laminar pattern. Modern parcellations (e.g., the Human Connectome Project's 180-area map per hemisphere) use multi-modal approaches combining architecture, connectivity, and function.
