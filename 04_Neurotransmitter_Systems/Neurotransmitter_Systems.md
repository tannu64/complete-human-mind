# Chapter 4: Neurotransmitter Systems

## 4.1 Overview

Neurotransmitters are endogenous chemical messengers that transmit signals across synapses. Over 100 neuroactive substances have been identified. They can be broadly classified into:

- **Small-molecule transmitters**: Amino acids, monoamines, acetylcholine, purines, gaseous transmitters
- **Neuropeptides**: Larger signaling molecules (3–40+ amino acids) often co-released with small-molecule transmitters

A molecule qualifies as a classical neurotransmitter if it: (1) is synthesized in the presynaptic neuron, (2) is stored in vesicles and released upon depolarization in a Ca²⁺-dependent manner, (3) produces a postsynaptic effect by binding specific receptors, and (4) has a mechanism for signal termination.

## 4.2 Amino Acid Transmitters

### Glutamate — The Primary Excitatory Transmitter

Glutamate mediates ~80% of all excitatory synaptic transmission in the CNS. It is synthesized from glutamine (via glutaminase) or from α-ketoglutarate (via transamination in the TCA cycle). Packaged into vesicles by vesicular glutamate transporters (VGLUT1–3).

**Ionotropic Receptors:**

| Receptor | Ion Permeability | Kinetics | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AMPA** (GluA1–4) | Na⁺, K⁺ (Ca²⁺ if lacking GluA2) | Fast (τ ~2–5 ms) | Mediates bulk of fast excitatory transmission; GluA2 subunit determines Ca²⁺ impermeability (RNA editing at Q/R site) |
| **NMDA** (GluN1 + GluN2A–D or GluN3) | Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺ | Slow (τ ~50–300 ms) | Voltage-dependent Mg²⁺ block; requires simultaneous glutamate + glycine/D-serine co-agonist binding; coincidence detector (presynaptic activity + postsynaptic depolarization); critical for LTP induction |
| **Kainate** (GluK1–5) | Na⁺, K⁺ | Intermediate | Modulates presynaptic release; present at mossy fiber–CA3 synapses; role in epileptogenesis |

**Metabotropic Receptors (mGluR1–8):**
- **Group I** (mGluR1, mGluR5): Postsynaptic; Gq-coupled → phospholipase C → IP3 + DAG → Ca²⁺ release + PKC. Enhance excitability; promote LTD at some synapses; involved in endocannabinoid signaling.
- **Group II** (mGluR2, mGluR3): Presynaptic and glial; Gi/Go-coupled → decrease cAMP → reduce glutamate release. Autoreceptor function; neuroprotective.
- **Group III** (mGluR4, mGluR6, mGluR7, mGluR8): Presynaptic; Gi/Go-coupled → reduce release. mGluR6 is essential for ON-bipolar cell signaling in the retina.

**Excitotoxicity**: Excessive glutamate release → sustained NMDA receptor activation → massive Ca²⁺ influx → activation of calpains, endonucleases, nitric oxide synthase, mitochondrial dysfunction → neuronal death. This is a major mechanism of damage in stroke, traumatic brain injury, and neurodegenerative disease. Normal prevention: astrocytic glutamate uptake via EAAT2 (responsible for ~90% of glutamate clearance).

### GABA (γ-Aminobutyric Acid) — The Primary Inhibitory Transmitter

GABA is synthesized from glutamate by **glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD65 and GAD67)**, which requires pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6) as a cofactor. Packaged by vesicular GABA transporter (VGAT/VIAAT).

**GABA-A Receptors**: Pentameric ligand-gated Cl⁻ channels (most common: 2α + 2β + 1γ subunit composition). There are 19 subunit genes (α1–6, β1–3, γ1–3, δ, ε, θ, π, ρ1–3), generating enormous diversity.
- Fast inhibition (τ ~10–30 ms phasic; tonic inhibition via extrasynaptic δ-containing receptors)
- **Binding sites**: GABA site (agonist: muscimol; antagonist: bicuculline); benzodiazepine site (at α/γ interface — allosteric positive modulator); barbiturate site; neurosteroid site; ethanol site (on δ-containing receptors); picrotoxin site (channel blocker)
- **Developmental note**: In immature neurons (embryonic/early postnatal), GABA is *excitatory* because high intracellular Cl⁻ concentration (due to NKCC1 expression > KCC2) makes ECl more positive than resting potential. The switch to inhibition occurs as KCC2 expression increases, lowering intracellular Cl⁻.

**GABA-B Receptors**: Obligate heterodimers (GABA-B1 + GABA-B2); Gi/Go-coupled GPCRs.
- Presynaptic: Reduce Ca²⁺ channel activity → decrease neurotransmitter release
- Postsynaptic: Activate GIRK K⁺ channels → slow, prolonged hyperpolarization (slow IPSP, τ ~100–300 ms)
- Agonist: baclofen (muscle relaxant)

**GABA metabolism**: Degraded by **GABA-transaminase (GABA-T)** to succinic semialdehyde, then to succinate (entering TCA cycle). Reuptake by GAT-1 (neuronal) and GAT-3 (astrocytic) transporters. Pharmacological inhibition of GAT-1 (tiagabine) or GABA-T (vigabatrin) increases GABAergic tone — used as anticonvulsants.

### Glycine

The primary inhibitory transmitter in the spinal cord and brainstem. Glycine receptors are pentameric Cl⁻ channels (α1–4, β subunits). Also serves as an obligate co-agonist at NMDA receptors (alongside D-serine). Reuptake by GlyT1 (glial — regulates NMDA co-agonist site) and GlyT2 (neuronal). Strychnine is a glycine receptor antagonist; strychnine poisoning causes disinhibition → convulsions.

## 4.3 Monoamine Transmitters

Monoamines share a common structural feature (an amino group connected to an aromatic ring by a two-carbon chain) and are synthesized from amino acid precursors.

### Dopamine (DA)

**Synthesis**: Tyrosine → L-DOPA (by tyrosine hydroxylase, TH — rate-limiting) → Dopamine (by DOPA decarboxylase/aromatic amino acid decarboxylase, AADC). Packaged by VMAT2.

**Pathways** (four major):
1. **Mesolimbic**: VTA → nucleus accumbens, amygdala, hippocampus. Reward, motivation, salience, addiction.
2. **Mesocortical**: VTA → prefrontal cortex. Working memory, executive function, cognitive control. Hypofunction → negative symptoms of schizophrenia; optimal function requires "inverted-U" dopamine levels.
3. **Nigrostriatal**: Substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc) → dorsal striatum (caudate/putamen). Motor control, habit formation. Degeneration → Parkinson's disease (~60–80% DA neuron loss before motor symptoms appear).
4. **Tuberoinfundibular**: Hypothalamic arcuate nucleus → median eminence → pituitary portal circulation. Inhibits prolactin release. Blockade by antipsychotics → hyperprolactinemia.

**Receptors** (all GPCRs):
- **D1-like family** (D1, D5): Gs-coupled → activate adenylyl cyclase → increase cAMP → PKA activation. Generally excitatory postsynaptic effects.
- **D2-like family** (D2, D3, D4): Gi/Go-coupled → inhibit adenylyl cyclase → decrease cAMP. Also activate GIRK channels (hyperpolarization) and inhibit presynaptic Ca²⁺ channels. D2 autoreceptors on DA neurons inhibit firing and release. D2 is the primary target of all clinically effective antipsychotics.

**Termination**: Reuptake by **DAT** (dopamine transporter; target of cocaine and methylphenidate/Ritalin). Degradation by **MAO-A/B** (intracellular) and **COMT** (extracellular, particularly important in PFC where DAT expression is low). COMT Val158Met polymorphism affects PFC dopamine levels and cognitive performance.

**Reward prediction error**: Dopamine neurons encode not reward itself, but **reward prediction error** (RPE) — the difference between expected and actual reward (Schultz, 1997). Unexpected reward → phasic burst firing. Expected reward → no change. Expected reward omission → phasic pause. This signal is mathematically equivalent to the temporal difference (TD) error in reinforcement learning algorithms.

### Serotonin (5-Hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT)

**Synthesis**: Tryptophan → 5-hydroxytryptophan (by tryptophan hydroxylase, TPH1 periphery / TPH2 brain — rate-limiting) → Serotonin (by AADC). Packaged by VMAT2. ~95% of body's serotonin is in the gut (enterochromaffin cells); CNS serotonin must be synthesized locally (does not cross BBB).

**Cell bodies**: **Raphe nuclei** (B1–B9 cell groups along the brainstem midline). Dorsal raphe (B7) and median raphe (B8) provide most ascending projections, innervating virtually the entire forebrain.

**Receptors**: 7 families, at least 14 subtypes — the most pharmacologically complex neurotransmitter system:
- **5-HT1A**: Gi-coupled. Presynaptic autoreceptor (raphe) → reduces 5-HT firing; postsynaptic (hippocampus, cortex) → hyperpolarization. Target of buspirone (anxiolytic). Desensitization of autoreceptors underlies the 2–4 week delay in SSRI antidepressant effect.
- **5-HT1B/1D**: Gi-coupled. Presynaptic terminal autoreceptors → reduce 5-HT release. Target of triptans (migraine treatment — vasoconstriction + reduced trigeminal nociception).
- **5-HT2A**: Gq-coupled. Cortical excitation; target of classical psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, DMT act as partial agonists); inverse agonism at 5-HT2A → atypical antipsychotic mechanism (clozapine).
- **5-HT2C**: Gq-coupled. Appetite suppression, mood regulation. Editing of 5-HT2C mRNA produces variants with altered function.
- **5-HT3**: The only ionotropic serotonin receptor (ligand-gated Na⁺/K⁺ channel). Fast excitation. Expressed on vagal afferents and in brainstem → nausea/emesis circuits. Target of ondansetron (antiemetic).
- **5-HT4, 5-HT6, 5-HT7**: Gs-coupled → increase cAMP. Roles in cognition (5-HT4, 5-HT6), circadian rhythm (5-HT7).

**Termination**: Reuptake by **SERT** (serotonin transporter; target of SSRIs — fluoxetine, sertraline, etc.; and MDMA/ecstasy which reverses SERT to release 5-HT). Degradation by MAO-A → 5-HIAA (measured in CSF as index of serotonergic activity).

### Norepinephrine (NE) / Noradrenaline

**Synthesis**: Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → **Norepinephrine** (by dopamine β-hydroxylase, DBH, inside vesicles). In the adrenal medulla, an additional step: NE → Epinephrine (by PNMT).

**Cell bodies**: **Locus coeruleus (LC)** — a small bilateral nucleus in the dorsal pons containing ~15,000–30,000 neurons per side in humans, yet projecting to virtually every brain region. The LC-NE system functions as a global "arousal dial."

**Receptors** (all GPCRs):
- **α1** (Gq): Postsynaptic excitation (smooth muscle contraction, cortical arousal)
- **α2** (Gi): Presynaptic autoreceptor → inhibits NE release; postsynaptic in PFC → enhances working memory (clonidine, guanfacine — used for ADHD). α2A receptors in PFC strengthen network connections via HCN channel inhibition.
- **β1** (Gs): Cardiac stimulation; amygdala → enhances emotional memory consolidation
- **β2** (Gs): Bronchodilation; glial glucose mobilization
- **β3** (Gs): Adipose tissue thermogenesis

**Termination**: Reuptake by **NET** (target of SNRIs — venlafaxine, duloxetine; atomoxetine for ADHD; cocaine also blocks NET). Degradation by MAO and COMT.

**LC-NE function**: The LC operates in two modes — **tonic** (sustained moderate firing → broad arousal, task engagement) and **phasic** (brief bursts in response to salient stimuli → focused attention, decision execution). The Aston-Jones model proposes that optimal cognitive performance occurs at intermediate tonic LC activity; too low → drowsiness, too high → anxiety/distractibility.

## 4.4 Acetylcholine (ACh)

**Synthesis**: Choline + Acetyl-CoA → ACh (by **choline acetyltransferase**, ChAT). Choline is obtained from dietary sources and recycled from ACh breakdown. Packaged by **VAChT**.

**CNS sources**:
- **Basal forebrain** (nucleus basalis of Meynert, medial septum, diagonal band): Projects to cortex and hippocampus → attention, learning, cortical activation. Severe degeneration in Alzheimer's disease.
- **Pedunculopontine and laterodorsal tegmental nuclei** (brainstem): Project to thalamus → REM sleep, arousal, locomotion.
- **Striatal cholinergic interneurons** (tonically active neurons, TANs): ~1–2% of striatal neurons but exert powerful modulation on MSN circuits; pause in response to reward-predicting stimuli.

**Receptors**:
- **Nicotinic (nAChR)**: Pentameric ligand-gated cation channels (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺). Major CNS subtypes: α4β2 (high affinity, widespread — primary target of nicotine addiction) and α7 (high Ca²⁺ permeability, rapid desensitization — hippocampus, cortex, immune cells). Muscle-type: (α1)2β1δε at the neuromuscular junction.
- **Muscarinic (mAChR)**: GPCRs, 5 subtypes:
  - M1 (Gq): Cortex, hippocampus → excitation, memory. Most important for cognitive enhancement.
  - M2 (Gi): Cardiac (vagal slowing); presynaptic autoreceptor
  - M3 (Gq): Smooth muscle contraction, glandular secretion
  - M4 (Gi): Striatum — modulates dopaminergic signaling
  - M5 (Gq): VTA — modulates dopamine release

**Termination**: Unique among neurotransmitters — **enzymatic degradation in the synaptic cleft** by **acetylcholinesterase (AChE)**, one of the fastest enzymes known (~25,000 ACh molecules hydrolyzed per second per molecule). Inhibitors of AChE (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) are used to treat Alzheimer's; organophosphate nerve agents (sarin) irreversibly inhibit AChE → cholinergic crisis.

## 4.5 Neuropeptides

Neuropeptides are larger signaling molecules synthesized in the soma (ribosomal translation of prepropeptide → cleavage → active peptide), packaged in **large dense-core vesicles** (LDCVs), and released from both terminals and dendrites typically during **high-frequency firing** (higher Ca²⁺ threshold than small-molecule vesicle release). They act via GPCRs with slower onset but longer-lasting effects (volume transmission; minutes to hours).

### Key Neuropeptide Systems

**Opioid peptides**: Endorphins (β-endorphin from POMC), enkephalins (met- and leu-enkephalin from proenkephalin), dynorphins (from prodynorphin). Act on mu (μ, MOR), delta (δ, DOR), and kappa (κ, KOR) opioid receptors — all Gi/Go-coupled → reduce cAMP, open K⁺ channels, close Ca²⁺ channels. Functions: pain modulation, reward (μ), dysphoria (κ), stress response.

**Endocannabinoids**: Lipid-derived retrograde messengers — **anandamide (AEA)** and **2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG)**. Synthesized on demand from membrane phospholipids (not stored in vesicles). Act primarily on **CB1 receptors** (Gi-coupled, densely expressed in cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, cerebellum) and **CB2 receptors** (primarily immune cells, microglia). CB1 activation on presynaptic terminals → depresses neurotransmitter release (both glutamate and GABA → depolarization-induced suppression of excitation/inhibition, DSE/DSI). Critical for synaptic plasticity, pain, appetite, and emotional regulation.

**Orexin/Hypocretin**: Orexin-A and orexin-B produced exclusively by ~70,000 neurons in the lateral hypothalamus. Project widely. OX1R and OX2R (Gq/Gs-coupled). Essential for maintaining wakefulness and stabilizing sleep-wake transitions. Loss of orexin neurons → narcolepsy type 1 (with cataplexy). Dual orexin receptor antagonists (suvorexant, lemborexant) used as sleep aids.

**Oxytocin and Vasopressin**: Synthesized in hypothalamic PVN and supraoptic nucleus; released from posterior pituitary (endocrine) and from dendrites (paracrine CNS effects). Oxytocin: social bonding, trust, maternal behavior, uterine contraction, milk letdown. Vasopressin: water retention (V2), vasoconstriction (V1a), social behavior and pair bonding (V1a in ventral pallidum), aggression.

**Neuropeptide Y (NPY)**: One of the most abundant brain peptides. Potent orexigenic (appetite-stimulating) signal via Y1 and Y5 receptors in hypothalamic arcuate nucleus. Also anxiolytic and anti-epileptic via Y1 receptors in amygdala and hippocampus. Co-released with GABA and norepinephrine.

**Substance P and neurokinins**: Tachykinin family. Substance P: pain transmission (released from C-fiber terminals in dorsal horn); neurogenic inflammation; emesis (NK1 receptor antagonist aprepitant = antiemetic).

**Cholecystokinin (CCK)**: Co-released with glutamate; CCK-B receptors in cortex and amygdala → anxiety, panic; CCK-A receptors → satiety signaling.

**Corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH)**: Hypothalamic PVN → anterior pituitary → ACTH release → cortisol (HPA axis). Also acts in amygdala and locus coeruleus to promote anxiety and arousal.

## 4.6 Other Neurotransmitters and Neuromodulators

### Histamine
Synthesized from histidine by histidine decarboxylase (HDC) exclusively in the **tuberomammillary nucleus (TMN)** of the posterior hypothalamus. Projects diffusely throughout the brain. Receptors: H1 (Gq — wakefulness, allergic response), H2 (Gs — gastric acid), H3 (Gi — presynaptic autoreceptor and heteroreceptor, widespread in CNS). TMN neurons are active during waking, silent during sleep. First-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine) that cross the BBB → sedation via H1 blockade.

### Purines
**ATP**: Released as co-transmitter from many neurons. Acts on ionotropic **P2X** receptors (1–7; cation channels — fast excitation, pain signaling) and metabotropic **P2Y** receptors (1–14; GPCRs). ATP is a major signaling molecule in astrocyte Ca²⁺ waves.

**Adenosine**: Generated from ATP breakdown (extracellular by ectonucleotidases). Acts on A1 (Gi — inhibitory, widespread, sleep-promoting), A2A (Gs — excitatory, enriched in striatum, arousal), A2B, A3 receptors. Adenosine accumulates during wakefulness (reflecting metabolic activity) and promotes sleep pressure — this is the homeostatic sleep drive. **Caffeine** is a competitive antagonist at A1 and A2A receptors, promoting wakefulness.

### Gaseous Transmitters
**Nitric oxide (NO)**: Synthesized by **neuronal nitric oxide synthase (nNOS)** from arginine; not stored in vesicles; diffuses freely across membranes. Activates soluble guanylyl cyclase → cGMP → PKG. Roles: retrograde signaling at synapses (modulates LTP), vasodilation (neurovascular coupling), and neuroimmune function. Excess NO contributes to excitotoxic damage.

**Carbon monoxide (CO)**: Produced by heme oxygenase (HO-2); similar signaling to NO via guanylyl cyclase. Neuromodulatory roles in olfactory neurons, hippocampus, and hypothalamus.

**Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S)**: Produced by cystathionine β-synthase (CBS) and 3-mercaptopyruvate sulfurtransferase (3-MST). Enhances NMDA receptor function; modulates LTP in hippocampus; vasoactive.

## 4.7 Neurotransmitter Co-transmission

The classical "one neuron, one transmitter" (Dale's principle in its strict form) is now recognized as the exception rather than the rule. Most neurons release multiple signaling molecules:

- Glutamate neurons may co-release GABA, dopamine, or zinc
- GABAergic interneuron subtypes are defined partly by co-released peptides: PV+ (no peptide), SST+ (somatostatin), VIP+ (vasoactive intestinal peptide), CCK+ (cholecystokinin), NPY+
- Monoamine neurons frequently co-release glutamate or GABA (e.g., VTA dopamine neurons co-release glutamate via VGLUT2)
- Small-molecule and neuropeptide co-transmission is the norm; they are released at different firing frequencies and act on different timescales

Co-transmission enables neurons to send qualitatively different signals depending on their firing rate and pattern — a fundamental mechanism for encoding information complexity.
